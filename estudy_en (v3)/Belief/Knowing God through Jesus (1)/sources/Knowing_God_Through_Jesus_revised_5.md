[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**EYEBROW:** Common Question
**TERM:** “Hasn’t the Bible been corrupted?”
**QUESTION:** You may have heard — or believe yourself — that the Bible has been corrupted and can no longer be trusted. What do you already know or think about this claim?
**ANSWER:** The manuscript evidence for the New Testament alone is extraordinary: over 5,800 Greek manuscripts survive, with thousands more in Latin and other languages. No other ancient document comes close to this level of attestation. When scholars compare these manuscripts, they find a text that is remarkably consistent. The variations that do exist are minor — spelling differences, word order — and no central Christian doctrine rests on a disputed reading.

But here is the more important question: *when* was the supposed corruption supposed to have happened? Jesus himself — whom the Quran also honours — quoted the Old Testament constantly as reliable Scripture. If the text was corrupted, it must have happened after Jesus. But many of the Old Testament manuscripts we have *predate* or are *contemporary with* Jesus’ time. The math simply doesn’t work.

Christians hold that God, who inspired the Scriptures, also preserved them. Not in a mechanical way that removes human involvement — but in a real way that ensures the message is trustworthy.

There is also a difficulty within the Islamic tradition itself: the Quran presents God as the guardian of the scriptures he has given. If the Bible was corrupted, a quiet but serious question arises — did God allow what he said he would protect to be altered? That question is worth pondering.

[type: callout; subtype: general]
**EYEBROW:** A Note on How to Read the Bible
**TERM:** Read it like a story, not a rulebook
**QUESTION:** How should a new believer begin reading the Bible?
**ANSWER:** The Bible is best understood as a grand story — Creation, Fall, Redemption, New Creation — with Jesus at the centre. It is not a legal code to be applied literally across all time, nor a series of isolated commands. Each part — a psalm, a prophecy, a letter — makes most sense when you understand its place in the whole story.

One thing worth knowing from the start: the Bible is a different kind of book from the Qur’an — not because it is less holy, but because God chose to speak through story, history, poetry, and letter, written by many different people across many centuries. Getting used to that is part of reading it well. Each book has its own voice, its own context, and its own place in the larger story. That is not a weakness — it is part of how God has chosen to speak.

The best place to start? There are many ways to start, but one good one is to start with **Mark’s Gospel.** It is short, urgent, and powerful. It moves fast — within a few chapters, Jesus has cast out demons, healed the sick, forgiven sin, and calmed a storm. Mark forces you to ask the same question the disciples asked: *Who is this man?*

Read Mark in one or two sittings if you can. Then come back to it again, and read it slowly.

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Before we move to personal reflection, here are the three key truths from this chapter to hold on to:

[type: text; subtype: intro]

Every person has a picture of God — even those who say they do not. That picture shapes everything: how we pray, how we feel when we fail, whether we draw near or keep our distance, whether we expect love or only judgement.

For many people, that picture is of a God who is vast, sovereign, and powerful — a King, a Judge, a Creator. Those things are absolutely true. But the Bible claims something more: that this same God, the maker of heaven and earth, is also *personal*. He can be known, not just feared. He draws near — he doesn’t only command from a distance.

This chapter isn’t about replacing what you already know of God’s greatness. It is about expanding it — because the God revealed in Jesus is greater *and* closer than any picture we could have formed on our own.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — The God Who Is Merciful and Near

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Psalm 103 is one of the most beloved poems in all of Scripture. It was written by David — a man who had known both the heights of God’s blessing and the depths of his own failure — and it is a sustained meditation on the character of God. What does God actually *like*? What are his instincts toward human beings?

The answer, from beginning to end, is: compassion.

There is something important to notice before we read. Many people assume that the God of the Bible and the God of the Qur’an have the same personality — that the difference is mainly about Jesus and the Trinity. But the character of God as the Bible describes him is itself different — not just a fuller version of the same picture, but genuinely surprising. The Qur’an teaches that God can forgive; but you can never be certain, before the Day of Judgement, whether he has forgiven *you*. The God of Psalm 103 is different: he does not just forgive in principle — he removes your sin completely, as far as east is from west (v12), and he wants you to know it. This chapter is about that God.

**TERM:** “Is the God of the Bible the same as Allah?”
**QUESTION:** You may have wondered: are Christians and Muslims talking about the same God? Think about it before tapping to read a response.
**ANSWER:** This is a question worth taking seriously rather than brushing aside. There is one Creator God — the God of Abraham, who made the heavens and the earth. In that sense, yes: Christians and Muslims are not worshipping different beings.

But the Bible makes claims about this God that go further than other traditions. It claims that God is not only Creator and King, but that he is personally relational by nature — that within the one God there is a relationship of Father, Son, and Spirit (we will explore this more in Chapters 3 and 4). And it claims that this God has acted decisively in history by coming in the person of Jesus.

The honest answer, then, is: *same essence, different character*. The Bible gives a much fuller and more surprising revelation of who that God is. Both traditions agree on what we might call God’s essence: he is eternal, all-powerful, the Creator, without beginning or end. Where the Bible goes further is in revealing his personal character — not just that he is powerful, but that he is love at his very core, and that this love was not something he needed creation to express. It was already flowing, from eternity, within the relationship of Father, Son, and Spirit. This series exists to help you explore that revelation.

**QUESTION:** To some, addressing God as “Father” feels presumptuous, or even disrespectful. What is your instinct when you hear this language? Think about it before tapping to read a response.
**ANSWER:** This reaction is understandable, and worth taking seriously. In some cultures, calling God “Father” sounds like a reduction of his greatness — as if we are making him too ordinary, too familiar.

But Jesus’ intention is the opposite. The Aramaic word he would have used — *Abba* — is a word of deep trust and intimacy, used by a child who knows they are loved and safe. It is not casual disrespect; it is the language of secure belonging.

Crucially, this is not a title we invented for God — it is the title *Jesus himself* uses, and it is the title he invites *us* to use. To call God Father is to accept the relationship he has offered, not to claim an equality we have earned.

In saying “Our Father,” Jesus is not making a biological claim. He is revealing something about God’s character: that the God who made the universe *wants* his people near. He is not a distant king who tolerates subjects — he is a Father who welcomes children.

[type: text; subtype: intro]

In every generation, people have had to answer the same question about Jesus: *Who is he, exactly?*

Some say he was a great moral teacher. Others say he was a prophet — one of the greatest. Some say he was a revolutionary, a healer, a wise man. Islam honours him as the Messiah, born of a virgin, a worker of miracles, and one of the greatest prophets who ever lived.

But the New Testament won’t let us settle for any of these answers. Not because they are entirely wrong — but because they don’t go far enough. The Gospels press us, again and again, toward a more unsettling and more wonderful conclusion.

It is worth knowing, before we go further, that following Jesus is not adopting a foreign religion. The faith you have entered has been alive in the lands of the Middle East since the first century — long before Islam existed. You are not leaving your world for someone else’s. You are returning to something ancient.

In this chapter, we are going to let the Gospel of Mark show us who Jesus is — not through abstract argument, but through what he *does* and what he *says*. Then we will read Colossians 1, where Paul draws together the theological meaning of it all.

By the end, we will be face to face with the question Jesus himself put to his disciples: *“But who do you say that I am?”* (Mark 8:29)

**EYEBROW:** Common Question
**TERM:** “The Bible never says ‘I am God, worship me.’”
**QUESTION:** You may have heard the argument that Jesus never directly claimed to be God — and that the idea was added later by his followers. What do you make of that argument before reading a response?
**ANSWER:** This is one of the most common and important objections, and it deserves a careful answer rather than a dismissal.

It is true that Jesus rarely makes direct, explicit statements of the form “I am God.” But the argument misunderstands how he actually communicated. Throughout the Gospels, Jesus makes claims and performs actions that, in the Jewish context of his day, could only mean one thing — and his listeners understood exactly what he was saying. That is precisely why they tried to stone him (John 10:33) and why the charge at his trial was blasphemy.

Consider what Jesus does in the Gospels:
- He forgives sins — which, as the teachers of the law correctly understood, only God can do (Mark 2:7)
- He accepts worship without correcting those who offer it (Matthew 14:33)
- He speaks with his own authority, not as a prophet relaying God’s words: *“You have heard it said... but I say to you”* (Matthew 5)
- He claims to be the Lord of the Sabbath — a day that belonged to God alone (Mark 2:28)
- He says: *“Before Abraham was, I am”* — using the divine name (John 8:58)

The claim is made through actions, titles, and patterns of speech — not a single formula. The question is not whether the exact words appear, but whether the meaning is clear. And for his first-century Jewish audience, it was unmistakeable.

In the previous chapter we saw who Jesus is through what he *does and says* — forgiving sin, commanding nature, bearing the title that belongs to God alone. Now we go deeper into a question that has puzzled, challenged, and astonished people ever since: *how can Jesus be fully God, if God is one?*

The honest answer is: this is a mystery. No one has resolved it in a single sitting, and we won’t either. But we can do something more useful: we can let Jesus himself speak. The words we will read come from the upper room on the night before the crucifixion — the most intimate teaching Jesus ever gave his disciples. In those hours, he spoke with unusual clarity about his relationship with the Father, and about the Spirit he was about to send.

The doctrine we call the Trinity is not a formula invented by church councils. It is rooted in what Jesus said and did. So we begin where we should always begin: with Scripture.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
One God — Not Three

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

Before we read the passages, it helps to clear away a common misunderstanding. The Christian teaching about the Trinity is frequently misunderstood — even by Christians. So let us state clearly what it is *not*, and what it *is*.

Christians do not believe in three gods. That is called tritheism, and the church has always rejected it. Christians believe that there is <b>one God</b> — the same one God affirmed in the Hebrew Scriptures: *“Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one”* (Deuteronomy 6:4). What the New Testament reveals — and what Jesus himself discloses — is that within this one God there is an eternal relationship: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Three distinct persons, one God.

This is not something Christians invented to explain a puzzle. It is something that *forced itself* upon the earliest followers of Jesus because of what they experienced: they were monotheists to their core, and yet they found themselves worshipping Jesus alongside the Father, and experiencing the Spirit as a living, divine presence. The Trinity is the only framework that does justice to all of that.

**[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Misunderstanding
**Term:** “Doesn’t the Quran say the Trinity is Father, Son, and Mary?”
**Question:** The Quran’s objection to the Trinity in Al-Ma’idah 5:116 seems to be directed at a triad of Father, Son, and Mary — not Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. How do Christians respond to that?
**Answer:** Christians respond by saying: we reject that triad too. The Bible nowhere teaches that Mary is part of the Godhead, and no mainstream Christian tradition has ever believed it. The Quran appears to be responding to a real confusion — in fact, a heresy — that existed among some Christians in 7th-century Arabia, where certain groups did venerate Mary in ways that went far beyond what Scripture taught — and may genuinely have appeared to elevate her to a divine status.

The Christian teaching is Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — not Father, Son, and Mary. On that specific point, the Quran’s objection and the Bible’s teaching are in agreement: Mary is a remarkable and honoured woman, but she is not God, and she is not part of the Trinity.

Knowing this can help clear the ground. When Muslims and Christians talk about the Trinity, they are not always talking about the same thing — and it helps to be clear about what Christians actually claim before engaging the question.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE / SUMMARY TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

The Trinity is a mystery — but it is a <em>revealed</em> mystery, not an invented one. Here are three things that Christians can say clearly:

**1. There is one God.** Christians are not polytheists. The God of the Old Testament and the God of the New Testament are the same God. He alone is Creator, Lord, and Saviour.

**2. Within that one God, there is an eternal relationship.** Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are genuinely distinct — the Father is not the Son, the Son is not the Spirit — but they are not three separate gods. They are one in being, one in will, and one in love.

**3. We know this because Jesus himself revealed it.** The Trinity is not the result of philosophical speculation. It is the result of listening carefully to what Jesus said and did. He spoke of the Father as distinct from himself. He promised to send the Spirit. He shared glory with the Father before creation. The Trinitarian shape of God is shown in Scripture — not imposed upon it.

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Was the Trinity invented at the Council of Nicaea in AD 325?”
**Question:** Many people believe the Trinity was a later addition to Christianity. Is that true?
**Answer:** The Council of Nicaea (AD 325) did not *invent* the Trinity — it *defended* it against those who wanted to subtract from it. The council was responding to a teacher called Arius who argued that the Son was a created being, subordinate to the Father. Nicaea said: no — the Son is of the same nature as the Father, just as Scripture teaches. The Trinitarian structure itself is found in the very earliest New Testament documents: 2 Corinthians 13:14 was written around AD 55, less than 25 years after the crucifixion. Jesus himself speaks in Trinitarian patterns throughout John 14–17. Nicaea clarified and defended what was already there. It did not create it.

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “How can God have a Son? Doesn’t that imply something physical?”
**Question:** The phrase “Son of God” can sound biological or physical. What does it actually mean?
**Answer:** This is an understandable concern, and it is important to answer it clearly. “Son of God” does not mean that God had a child through any physical process. It is not that kind of language.

Arabic actually has two different words that are relevant and helpful here. *Walad* refers to a biological offspring — a child born through physical means. *Ibn* refers to a son in the relational, originating sense — one who comes from another, bears their nature, and stands in close relationship. The Quran’s objection to the idea of God having a son is specifically to the *walad* — and Christians agree completely: that is not what is meant. The Bible’s claim is of the *ibn* kind: Jesus is the one who originates from the very being of God, shares his nature, and stands in eternal relationship with the Father.

The Nicene Creed, written in the fourth century to address exactly this question, puts it this way: Jesus is *“begotten, not made.”* That phrase is worth reflecting on. *Begotten* means he comes from the Father — he shares the Father’s nature. *Not made* means he is not a creation, not something that came into existence at a point in time. He has always existed in relationship with the Father. “Son of God” names that relationship — the one who eternally proceeds from God’s own being, not the one born through any physical act.

There is something else worth noticing. The Quran itself calls Jesus *kalimatuhu* — “his Word” (An-Nisa 4:171). The Bible explains what that means: not a word written in a book, but the living Word who proceeded from God’s very being and entered the world as a person. “Son of God” and “Word of God” are two ways of pointing at the same truth — that Jesus comes from God, shares God’s nature, and is not a created being like any other.

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

Before we move to reflection, here is one reason why the Trinity is not just a puzzle to wrestle with — but a comfort to rest in.

The Bible says that God is love (1 John 4:8). But love, by its nature, requires someone to love. If God were a single, solitary person before creation existed, he could not have been love — he would have needed the world in order to have someone to love. That would mean God *needed* us to be fully himself.

The Trinity means something entirely different: God’s love did not begin when he made the world. It was already flowing, from eternity, between Father, Son, and Spirit — a relationship of self-giving love that has always existed. God did not create us because he needed love. He created us because he *is* love, and love overflows. Love is not an afterthought, relationships are not an afterthought. Relationships are central to who God is, and to all of reality. Love is central to who God is, and to all of reality.

This is also why Jesus could pray in John 17:24: *“You loved me before the creation of the world.”* The love that reached you is not new. It is eternal — and you have been welcomed into it.

<!-- CHAPTER INTRODUCTION -->

**[type: text; subtype: intro]**

Imagine a doctor who tells you your symptoms are minor — that you simply need to rest more and try harder. You follow the advice. But the pain keeps returning, because the doctor missed the real diagnosis. The problem was never your behaviour. It was something deeper that no amount of effort could fix.

Many people who have taken their faith seriously describe exactly this kind of restlessness — a sense that something is unresolved even after sincere religious practice. They prayed faithfully, fasted carefully, tried to obey diligently — and still something felt wrong at a level that effort couldn’t reach. If that is part of your experience, the Bible has a name for it. The problem is not a failure of devotion. It is that the diagnosis was incomplete.

That is what this chapter is for. Many of us arrive with a deeply ingrained instinct: the fundamental problem between us and God is what we *do* — specific sins, particular failures. And the solution, it follows, must be to *do better*: pray more, resist more, discipline ourselves more strictly.

But the Bible tells a different story. The problem is not simply what we do. It is what we *are* — what has gone wrong at the very centre of who we are. Before the gospel can be good news, we need to understand clearly what it is good news *about*.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
A Framework: Two Ways of Understanding the Problem

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE / SUMMARY TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

It may help to set two contrasting understandings of sin alongside each other — not to argue, but to see clearly what the Bible is actually saying.

**A common instinct:**
The human being is essentially capable — we know what is right; we simply lose sight of it and need to be reminded. Sin is forgetfulness, drifting, a failure to obey clearly enough. The fix, therefore, is to try harder, do more, discipline yourself more carefully. If the problem is forgetting, then the solution is reminding. A prophet who speaks God’s words and calls us back to him makes perfect sense as the solution. With enough effort and the right guidance, the gap can be closed.

**What the Bible teaches:**
Sin is a condition of the heart — a disordering of who we are at our deepest level. It is not simply that we forget God from time to time; it is that something has gone wrong at the root. We are not just forgetful — we are, as Romans 3 puts it, unable to seek God at all on our own. Because the problem runs that deep, the solution must come from *outside* us entirely. A broken compass cannot be fixed by giving it better directions — it needs to be repaired from the inside. That is why what is needed is not a reminder but a rescue, not a messenger pointing the way, but a saviour who carries us there.

A life of careful obedience — where each act must be correct, where the outcome is never certain until the end — is an exhausting place to live. The Bible does not dismiss that effort. But it offers something different: not a higher standard to reach, but a gift to receive.

This is not meant to discourage. It is meant to liberate. When we see clearly that we cannot save ourselves, we are ready to receive the salvation that God offers as a gift.

# Chapter 6 — What Did Jesus Do?
## The Cross and the Resurrection

---

<!-- ============================================================
     ELEMENT TAGS KEY
     Each section is prefixed with a tag indicating the
     recommended JSON element type/subtype from chapterTemplateV2.

[type: text; subtype: intro]

Something unexpected happens at the centre of the Christian story. After everything we have learned — about who God is, who Jesus is, the depth of what sin does — we arrive here: a cross. Not a throne. Not a victory parade. A crucifixion.

For many people, this is the hardest part of the gospel. Why would God allow this? Why would Jesus — Son of God, Lord of creation — die like a criminal? What could a death two thousand years ago possibly have to do with us today?

This chapter sits at the very heart of the Christian faith. Everything before it has been preparation. Everything after it flows from what happened here. We will look at two things: what Jesus did on the cross, and what God did three days later. Together, they change everything.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding; eyebrow: “Common Misunderstanding”; term: “Did Jesus really die on the cross?”]

The Quran (Surah 4:157) says that Jesus was not crucified — that it only appeared so to those who saw it. This is worth thinking carefully about.

The crucifixion of Jesus is one of the most historically well-documented events of the ancient world. It is recorded not only in the Gospels but in non-Christian sources — including the Roman historian Tacitus (writing c. AD 116) and the Jewish historian Josephus (c. AD 93). The disciples who proclaimed the resurrection did so at great personal cost — many were imprisoned or killed for this claim. People do not die for what they know to be false.

The Christian claim is not that something happened *to* Jesus against his will — but that he went to the cross *deliberately*, as an act of rescue. It was not defeat. It was the mission.

**“The Quran says Jesus was not crucified — someone else was made to look like him.”**
Acknowledge this with respect. Then note: the crucifixion is one of the most historically attested events of the ancient world, referenced in Roman and Jewish sources outside the Bible. 1 Corinthians 15:3–8 records an early creed — scholars date the tradition it preserves to within a few years of the events themselves. Paul lists named witnesses who were still alive. The tomb was empty — a fact his opponents never disputed, only tried to explain away. The disciples were transformed from terrified fugitives into fearless proclaimers who turned the world upside down. Something happened.

# Chapter 7 — How Are We Saved? *Grace, Not Works*

> **Key Texts:** Ephesians 2:1–10 · Luke 18:9–14 · Romans 4:4–5
> **Big Idea:** We are saved by grace through faith — not by works. Salvation is a gift to receive, not an achievement to earn.

---

## Chapter Introduction

[type: text; subtype: intro]

Most of us arrive at faith carrying a deep instinct: *God’s acceptance must be earned.* You prayed five times a day. You fasted through Ramadan. You gave to those in need. Each of these was an act of genuine devotion — and somewhere at the back of your mind, each one was also part of the account you were hoping would balance in your favour.

It is a deeply human way to think about God. And in many traditions, it makes complete sense: if the human being is essentially capable — not broken, just forgetful, in need of guidance and discipline — then working harder really ought to be enough. That is a hopeful view of human nature. But the Bible sees the problem differently. And if the problem is different from what we thought, then the solution will be too.

But in this chapter, Jesus and Paul are going to say something that cuts straight across that instinct — something that at first sounds almost too good to be true, and then, slowly, begins to feel like the most liberating thing you have ever heard.

Salvation is not an achievement. It is a gift.

We are not saved because we were good enough. We are saved because God was gracious enough. And that changes everything — including what it means to live well before him.

## Section 5 — So What Does This Mean for Assurance?

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**So What Does This Mean for Assurance?**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

For many people — especially those who have grown up in a tradition that emphasises judgment and uncertainty — one of the most unsettling aspects of the Christian gospel is this: *you can know that you are saved.*

In some frameworks, the verdict on a person’s life is hidden until the very end — revealed only when deeds are weighed and the final accounting is made. No one can know in advance how that will go. The faithful can only hope, and keep trying.

The Christian gospel offers something different — not because it takes sin less seriously, but because it takes the cross more seriously. Assurance is not based on the weight of your deeds. It is based on what Christ has already done, and on God’s own promise. Some of you may have hoped that the Prophet would intercede for you on that final day. The Christian claim is that Jesus is himself both Judge and Saviour — and that his intercession is not a hope but a certainty, secured by what he has already accomplished.

Not arrogantly. Not because you are better than others. But because you are trusting a promise made by God himself, sealed in the blood of Christ, and confirmed by the Holy Spirit.

This is not a claim about your goodness. It is a claim about his faithfulness.

For many people, the longing for this kind of assurance is not primarily intellectual. It is the quiet exhaustion of a life spent trying to be enough — praying enough, obeying enough, atoning enough — and never quite knowing if the account will balance. That exhaustion is real. The gospel does not dismiss it. It addresses it: not by demanding more effort, but by offering a certainty that effort could never produce.

## Big Picture Truths

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**Big Picture Truths**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Three truths to hold clearly from this chapter:

**Grace** is not God lowering his standards. It is God, at great cost to himself, meeting us where we could never reach him. The cross shows that sin was taken seriously — completely seriously — and yet forgiven completely.

**Faith** is not a religious work we perform to earn God’s favour. It is the open hand that receives a gift. It is trust. It is dependence. It is saying: *I cannot do this. I am relying entirely on what Christ has done.*

**Works** matter — enormously. But they flow from salvation; they do not produce it. Ephesians 2:10 says we are created in Christ Jesus *for* good works. We do not work in order to be accepted. We work *because* we are accepted.

**Term:** “Isn’t certainty about salvation arrogant?”
**Question:** Is it proud to claim assurance of salvation?

If a trusted friend promises to meet you and you say ‘I trust that he will come,’ that is not arrogance — it is confidence in *his* reliability, not your own. Christian assurance is the same: it rests on what God has promised, not on what we have achieved. This is quite different from any framework where the outcome remains hidden until the final day. ## Section 2 — The Spirit Who Says ‘Father’

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**The Spirit Who Says ‘Father’**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Romans 8 is one of the great chapters of the New Testament — a sustained description of what life in the Spirit looks like for those who belong to Christ. Paul does not speak of the Christian life as merely a new set of obligations or a change of religious allegiance. He speaks of it as a family relationship, confirmed from the inside by the Spirit of God himself.

Before we read, it is worth naming something honestly. For many people who come to faith from a Muslim background, calling God “Father” is not simply a new theological idea — it is personally difficult. It can feel presumptuous. Disrespectful. Even ridiculous — like reaching for a word that belongs to a different kind of relationship than the one you have known. The distance between “Lord and Master” and “Abba” is not just doctrinal; it is emotional, and it can take time to cross.

## A Reflection on What Has Been Lost — and What Has Been Given

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**A Reflection on What Has Been Lost — and What Has Been Given**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

It would be dishonest to end this chapter without naming what many in this room may be feeling — a grief that sits alongside the joy.

The family of God is real. The access to the Father is real. The belonging among brothers and sisters in Christ is real.

And the losses are also real.

Parents who no longer speak to you. Siblings who feel you have betrayed them. A community that once was home. These are not small things, and this chapter does not ask you to pretend they are.

What the ummah provided was real: a sense of belonging, a shared identity, a community that knew you and held you. What the church offers is not less than that — it is the same deep needs met on different grounds. Not birth and blood and law, but grace and adoption and the Spirit. You have not exchanged belonging for isolation. You have exchanged one kind of family for another that will outlast it.

One specific loss worth naming is the rhythm of the year. Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha — these were not just religious obligations. They were seasons that shaped time, brought people together, and marked the year with meaning. When you can no longer participate in them fully, the year can feel strangely empty.

It is worth knowing that the Christian faith has its own rhythm of the year — a pattern that shapes every season around the story of Jesus. Advent and Christmas, when we mark his coming. Lent, a season of reflection leading to Good Friday. Easter, the celebration of his resurrection. Pentecost, when the Spirit was given. These are not merely dates in a diary; they are an invitation to live the whole year inside the story of what God has done. That rhythm is yours now — a new sacred pattern to inhabit, and one shared by Christians across the world and across the centuries.

What Scripture says is this: you are not starting from nothing. You are not an orphan searching for a home. You have been born into the largest and most permanent family in existence — a family that spans every nation and every century, and will outlast every earthly relationship. And you have a Father who has never once turned away from you.

Jesus himself said: “Truly I tell you, there is no one who has left home or brothers or sisters or mother or father or children or fields for my sake and for the gospel’s sake who will not receive a hundred times as much now in the present age — homes, brothers, sisters, mothers, children and fields — and in the age to come, eternal life.” *(Mark 10:29–30)*

That is not a denial of the cost. It is a promise made by the one who himself was rejected, abandoned, and alone — and who knows exactly what it costs to follow him.

## Big Picture Truths

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**Big Picture Truths**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Three truths to hold clearly from this chapter:

**You are a child of God** — not by effort, not by birth, not by religious performance, but by grace. John 1:12 says you have been *given the right* to be called God’s child. That right cannot be revoked.

**The Holy Spirit confirms your belonging** — from the inside. Romans 8:16 says the Spirit himself testifies that you are God’s child. Your sense of belonging is not based only on what you have been told; it is sealed by the living presence of God within you.

**You have a new family** — real, permanent, and global. The church is not a meeting to attend; it is a community to belong to. And it stretches back two thousand years and across every nation on earth — millions of people in every generation who have called the same God Father, trusted the same Saviour, and been shaped by the same Spirit. You have not joined a small or fragile thing. Belonging requires presence, investment, and the willingness to be known.

That family is made up of imperfect people still being shaped by grace — and it will sometimes disappoint you, as you will sometimes disappoint it. That is not a reason to withdraw. It is an invitation to stay, and to extend toward others the same grace that has been shown to you.

## Common Questions

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]

**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Does becoming a Christian mean I have to abandon my family?”
**Question:** Does faith in Christ require cutting ties with family?
**Answer:** No — and in fact, Jesus calls his followers to honour their parents and love those closest to them. Coming to faith may create conflict and distance; but that distance is not something believers choose or seek. Many find that over time, living with gentleness, love, and integrity before their families becomes one of the most powerful forms of witness. The new family of God supplements and sustains you; it does not ask you to stop loving those you were born to.

---

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]

**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Am I now considered an apostate?”
**Question:** Does Islam teach that I am an apostate — and what does that mean for me?
**Answer:** This is a question many carry silently, and it deserves an honest answer. Leaving Islam is indeed described in some Islamic traditions as apostasy (ridda), and in certain contexts this carries serious social, legal, or even physical consequences. It is right to acknowledge that reality honestly, and leaders should be available to speak privately with anyone facing genuine danger.

It is worth knowing that the Quran itself does not prescribe a worldly punishment for leaving Islam — it speaks of divine judgment in the afterlife. The harsher rulings come from later legal tradition, and were historically tied to political defection as much as personal faith. This does not remove the very real pressure many face from family or community. But it means the picture is more complex than it is sometimes presented.

What matters most in this moment is not what a community has decided to call you — but what God calls you. And the answer to that question is the one this entire chapter has been about: *child*. Beloved. Belonging. Nothing that any court or community declares can undo what God has declared over you in Christ.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]

**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Is ‘Abba’ the same as ‘Allah’?”
**Question:** Christians call God ‘Father’ or ‘Abba’ — is that the same as calling him ‘Allah’?
**Answer:** ‘Allah’ is simply the Arabic word for God — the same word Arab Christians use when they pray. ‘Abba’ is not a different God; it is a different *address* to the same God — one that reflects the intimacy of the relationship Jesus opened up. The Christian claim is not that a different God exists, but that the one God — Creator of all things — has revealed himself as Father through Jesus, and invites his people into that relationship. This is not a diminishing of God’s greatness; it is an expansion of our understanding of his nature.

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: “Application Challenge”; term: “Carrying This Forward”]

As this series closes, consider three things to carry with you:

**One truth to anchor you:** Choose a verse from this series — perhaps Romans 8:38–39, or John 15:9, or Romans 5:8 — and commit it to memory. Let it be the thing you return to when pressure comes.

**One relationship to nurture:** Identify one person — a fellow believer, a leader, a trusted friend in faith — with whom you will stay in regular contact. You were not made to walk this road alone.

**One step of witness:** Think of one person in your life who does not yet know Jesus. You do not have to argue with them or convert them. When the moment comes to share your faith with family or friends, resist the urge to begin by explaining what is wrong with what you have left behind. Lead with what you have found — the love, the forgiveness, the certainty of belonging. Let them ask the harder questions in their own time. For now, simply pray for that person by name, daily, and be ready to answer if they ever ask about the hope you carry.

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: “Common Questions — Leader Notes”; term: “Anticipated Questions This Chapter”]

**“What if my family finds out and things get much worse?”**
Do not minimise this fear — it is real and the consequences can be severe. Walk through 1 Peter 3:13–16 together and affirm that Jesus does not ask for recklessness. Wisdom about timing and context is not cowardice. At the same time, gently affirm that living in sustained secrecy has its own cost — spiritually and emotionally. Pray together specifically for wisdom about how and when to speak. If practical safety needs exist, address them concretely.

**“I don’t feel confident enough to tell anyone about my faith — I still have too many doubts.”**
This is worth addressing warmly. Peter does not say to be ready to give a *complete theological account* — he says to be ready to explain “the hope you possess.” You do not need to have resolved every question to speak about what has changed in you. Doubt and witness can coexist. In fact, honest witness from a place of wrestling is often more compelling than polished certainty.

**“What about my Muslim family and friends — are they condemned?”**
Handle this with great care and tenderness. Avoid giving a cold doctrinal answer to what is a deeply personal question. Acknowledge that this is one of the hardest parts of the Christian journey for people in this group. Affirm what is true: God is the judge, not us. Our calling is to love, to pray, and to live in a way that makes Jesus visible. Direct participants toward Romans 9–11 for Paul’s own wrestling with this for his own people.

**“Is this the end — what happens now?”**
This is an important pastoral moment. Be clear about what ongoing support, community, and discipleship looks like beyond this series. If there is a church community, small group, or one-to-one mentoring available, name it concretely. The transition out of a structured series can feel destabilising. Make sure no one leaves without a clear next step and a known person to contact.

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: “For Leaders — Closing This Series”; term: “How to End Well”]

This final chapter deserves more than a normal ending. Consider:

- **Reading Romans 8:31–39 aloud together** as an act of declaration and worship — slowly, with weight. Let it be the last thing the group hears as a group.
- **Praying for each person by name** — specifically, with reference to what you know of their situation and journey. This is a powerful act of pastoral love.
- **Exchanging contact details** so that no one is isolated after the series ends.
- **Affirming what you have witnessed** in each person — their courage, their questions, their growth. Name it. It matters.

Romans 8:38–39
