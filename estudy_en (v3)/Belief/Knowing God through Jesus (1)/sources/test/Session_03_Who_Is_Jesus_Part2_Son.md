[type: text; subtype: intro]

In every generation, people have had to answer the same question about Jesus: *Who is he, exactly?*

Some say he was a great moral teacher. Others say he was a prophet — one of the greatest. Some say he was a revolutionary, a healer, a wise man. Islam honours him as the Messiah, born of a virgin, a worker of miracles, and one of the greatest prophets who ever lived.

But the New Testament will not let us settle for any of these answers alone. Not because they are entirely wrong — but because they do not go far enough. The Gospels press us, again and again, toward a more unsettling and more wonderful conclusion.

This session, we are going to let the Gospel of Mark show us who Jesus is — not through abstract argument, but through what Jesus *does* and what he *says*. We will then read Colossians 1, where Paul draws together the theological meaning of it all.

By the end, we will be face to face with a question Jesus himself asked his disciples: *"But who do you say that I am?"* (Mark 8:29)

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**EYEBROW:** Common Question
**TERM:** "The Bible never says 'I am God, worship me.'"
**QUESTION:** You may have heard the argument that Jesus never directly claimed to be God — and that the idea was added later by his followers. What do you make of that argument before reading a response?
**ANSWER:** This is one of the most common and important objections, and it deserves a careful answer rather than a dismissal.

It is true that Jesus rarely makes direct, explicit statements of the form "I am God." But the argument misunderstands how he actually communicated. Throughout the Gospels, Jesus makes claims and performs actions that, in the Jewish context of his day, could only mean one thing — and his listeners understood exactly what he was saying. That is precisely why they tried to stone him (John 10:33) and why the charge at his trial was blasphemy.

Consider what Jesus does in the Gospels:
- He forgives sins — which, as the teachers of the law correctly understood, only God can do (Mark 2:7)
- He accepts worship without correcting those who offer it (Matthew 14:33)
- He speaks with his own authority, not as a prophet relaying God's words: *"You have heard it said... but I say to you"* (Matthew 5)
- He claims to be the Lord of the Sabbath — a day that belonged to God alone (Mark 2:28)
- He says: *"Before Abraham was, I am"* — using the divine name (John 8:58)

The claim is made through actions, titles, and patterns of speech — not a single formula. The question is not whether the exact words appear, but whether the meaning is clear. And for his first-century Jewish audience, it was unmistakeable.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — The Theological Summary: Colossians 1:15–20

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Mark shows us *who Jesus is* through what he does. Paul, writing to the church in Colossae around AD 60, steps back and gives us the theological framework — who Jesus is in relation to God, creation, and the universe itself.

What follows is one of the most elevated passages in the entire New Testament. It may originally have been an early Christian hymn — which means these words were being sung by believers within thirty years of the resurrection.

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Notice the movement across this session's three passages:

In **Mark 1**, God himself identifies Jesus as his Son before Jesus begins his ministry.
In **Mark 2**, Jesus demonstrates the authority to do what only God can do — forgive sin.
In **Colossians 1**, Paul draws the full conclusion: Jesus is not merely a great man whom God used, but the one through whom and for whom everything was made — God himself, made visible in a human life.

This is why the question of Jesus' identity is not one Christians can leave open as a polite uncertainty. Everything else in the Christian faith depends on the answer.

That second question is not a historical curiosity. It is a live question — and it is being asked of you.

[type: heading; subtype: section]
**TEXT:** Big Picture Truths

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Here are the three key truths from this session to hold on to:

**1. Jesus' identity is revealed through what he does, not only what he says.** He forgives sins. He commands demons and storms. He speaks with his own authority. His actions are the claims — and they add up to one conclusion.

**2. Jesus is the image of the invisible God.** Paul's language in Colossians 1 is as high as language can go: Jesus is before all things, the agent of creation, the one who holds everything together. To know Jesus is to know what God is like.

**3. The question is personal, not just theological.** "Who do you say that I am?" is Jesus' own question, addressed to every person who encounters him. It cannot be answered by proxy. It requires a response from you.

[type: text; subtype: closing]

The Jesus of the New Testament is not a safe, manageable figure. He forgives sins that only God can forgive. He is described as the one through whom the universe was made. He asks questions that demand a personal answer.

But this same Jesus is also the one who stops for a paralysed man lowered through a roof. Who reaches out to those who are overlooked. Who, as we will see in later sessions, goes to a cross for the sake of people who have turned away from God.

His greatness and his tenderness belong together — just as we saw in Session 2 with God the Father. And that is no coincidence: *"Anyone who has seen me has seen the Father."* (John 14:9)

In Session 4, we will go deeper into the question of the Trinity — how Jesus relates to the Father and to the Spirit, and what it means that God is one.
