# Session 7 — How Are We Saved? *Grace, Not Works*

> **Key Texts:** Ephesians 2:1–10 · Luke 18:9–14 · Romans 4:4–5
> **Big Idea:** We are saved by grace through faith — not by works. Salvation is a gift to receive, not an achievement to earn.

---

## Chapter Introduction

[type: text; subtype: intro]

Most of us arrive at faith carrying a deep instinct: *God's acceptance must be earned.* Pray faithfully. Fast sincerely. Obey carefully. Do enough, and perhaps — *perhaps* — the scales will tip in your favour.

It is a deeply human way to think about God. And for those of us who have come from a background of careful religious practice, it runs even deeper. We have been formed by it. We have been honoured for it.

But in this session, Jesus and the apostle Paul are going to say something that cuts across that instinct entirely — something that at first sounds almost too good to be true, and then, slowly, begins to feel like the most liberating thing you have ever heard.

Salvation is not an achievement. It is a gift.

We are not saved because we were good enough. We are saved because God was gracious enough. And that changes everything — including what it means to live well before him.

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**What Has God Done?**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Ephesians 2 doesn't stop with the bad news. In fact, Paul seems almost unable to contain himself as he turns to what God has done. Notice how the sentence changes — not 'but we tried harder,' not 'but we improved.' The subject changes entirely. *But God.*

**Translation:** NET

[type: question; subtype: header]

**Header:** "But God…"
**Question:** What three things does Paul say about God in verse 4 that explain *why* he acted? What do those words — mercy, great love, richness — tell us about God's character?

---

[type: question; subtype: standard]

Paul says God 'made us alive together with Christ — even though we were dead.' What does it tell you about grace that God acted when we were dead, not when we were improving?

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

There is a small phrase tucked into the middle of verse 5 that Paul cannot seem to help interrupting himself to say: *by grace you are saved!* He is so eager to say it that he says it here, before he has even finished the sentence — and then says it again in verse 8. It is as though he cannot let you miss it.

---

[type: biblePassage]

**Bible Reference:** Ephesians 2:8–10
**Translation:** NET
**Passage Text:**
> ⁸ For by grace you are saved through faith, and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God; ⁹ it is not from works, so that no one can boast. ¹⁰ For we are his workmanship, having been created in Christ Jesus for good works that God prepared beforehand so we may do them.

---

[type: question; subtype: standard]

Verse 10 is sometimes missed. Paul does not say works are unimportant — he says we were *created for* good works. What is the difference between works that *earn* salvation and works that *flow from* salvation?

**Deeper:** [type: question; subtype: deeper]
If good works cannot save us but are still essential to the Christian life, how does that change the motivation behind doing them? What would it feel like to serve God out of love rather than fear of judgment?

## Section 3 — A Story That Turns Everything Upside Down

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**A Story That Turns Everything Upside Down**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Jesus was a master teacher who often used stories to deliver truth more powerfully than a lecture ever could. The parable he tells in Luke 18 was particularly shocking to its original audience — and it is worth letting it be shocking to us too. Two men. One religious, devoted, and serious. One a notorious public sinner. One of them goes home right with God.

It may not be the one you expect.

## Section 4 — Faith, Not Works: What Romans Says

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**Faith, Not Works: What Romans Says**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

In Romans 4, Paul draws on one of the most respected figures in the whole of religious history — Abraham. The father of the faithful. The one called 'Friend of God.' If anyone had earned their standing before God, surely it was Abraham. But Paul makes a startling argument.

---

[type: biblePassage]

**Bible Reference:** Romans 4:4–5
**Translation:** NET
**Passage Text:**
> ⁴ Now to the one who works, his pay is not credited as a gift but as what is owed. ⁵ But to the one who does not work, but believes in the one who declares the ungodly righteous, his faith is credited as righteousness.

**Passage URL:** https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Romans+4&version=NET

---

[type: question; subtype: standard]

Paul uses the language of wages and gifts. What is the difference between a wage and a gift? Which one is salvation, according to this passage?

---

[type: question; subtype: standard]

Paul says God 'declares the ungodly righteous.' Does that strike you as strange, or even unfair? What makes it possible for God to do this without ignoring sin?

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

This is where Sessions 5, 6, and 7 fit together. Session 5 showed us the depth of the problem — sin runs deeper than outward behaviour; it is a condition of the heart. Session 6 showed us the answer — Jesus took that sin upon himself at the cross, and the resurrection declares that his death achieved everything God intended. And now, in Session 7, we see how that applies to us: *by grace, through faith.* Not by performance. Through trust.

The cross is what makes it possible for God to declare sinners righteous without ignoring sin. The penalty has been paid. The gift is free.

## Section 5 — So What Does This Mean for Assurance?

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**So What Does This Mean for Assurance?**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

For many people — especially those who have grown up in a tradition that emphasises judgment and uncertainty — one of the most unsettling aspects of the Christian gospel is this: *you can know that you are saved.*

Not arrogantly. Not because you are better than others. But because you are trusting a promise made by God himself, sealed in the blood of Christ, and confirmed by the Holy Spirit.

This is not a claim about your goodness. It is a claim about his faithfulness.

---

[type: question; subtype: standard]

How do you feel about the idea of being *certain* of God's acceptance? Does it feel freeing, or does it make you uneasy? Why?

---

[type: question; subtype: standard]

If assurance is based on God's promise and not on our performance, what happens to that assurance when we sin? Does it have to be re-earned?

**Deeper:** [type: question; subtype: deeper]
John writes: "I have written these things to you who believe in the name of the Son of God so that you may know that you have eternal life" (1 John 5:13). Why do you think John considers it important that we *know*, not merely hope? What difference would that certainty make to the way you pray, or live?

## Big Picture Truths

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**Big Picture Truths**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Three truths to hold clearly from this session:

**Grace** is not God lowering his standards. It is God, at great cost to himself, meeting us where we could never reach him. The cross shows that sin was taken seriously — completely seriously — and yet forgiven completely.

**Faith** is not a religious work we perform to earn God's favour. It is the open hand that receives a gift. It is trust. It is dependence. It is saying: *I cannot do this. I am relying entirely on what Christ has done.*

**Works** matter — enormously. But they flow from salvation; they do not produce it. Ephesians 2:10 says we are created in Christ Jesus *for* good works. We do not work in order to be accepted. We work *because* we are accepted.

## Common Questions

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**Common Questions**

---

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]

**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** "Doesn't this mean we can do whatever we want?"
**Question:** If we're saved by grace and not by works, doesn't that remove any reason to live morally? Think about it before tapping 'See response.'
**Answer:** Paul addresses this directly in Romans 6. Grace is not a licence for careless living — it is a power that changes us from the inside. Those who have truly received grace find that their desires begin to change. They want to please God — not to earn his favour, but because they love him. A child who knows they are truly loved by their parent does not think: 'Great, I can do anything.' They want to make their parent glad. Grace produces that kind of love.

---

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]

**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** "In Islam, only God knows on the Day of Judgement whether you are saved. Isn't certainty arrogant?"
**Question:** Is it proud to claim assurance of salvation?
**Answer:** Christian assurance is not based on our own goodness — if it were, it would indeed be arrogant. It is based entirely on God's promise in Christ. Trusting a promise is not pride; it is faith. If a trusted friend promises to meet you and you say 'I trust that he will come,' that is not arrogance — it is confidence in *his* reliability, not your own. Christian assurance is the same: it rests on what God has promised, not on what we have achieved.

---

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]

**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** "What about good deeds — do they count for nothing?"
**Question:** Does grace make good deeds irrelevant?
**Answer:** Not at all. Ephesians 2:10 says we are 'created in Christ Jesus to do good works.' Good deeds matter enormously — but they are the fruit of salvation, not its root. Think of it this way: a tree does not produce fruit in order to *become* a fruit tree. It produces fruit *because* it already is one. In the same way, good works do not make us acceptable to God — they flow from those who have already been made acceptable, by grace.

## Closing Summary

[type: text; subtype: closing]

This session has been about the very heart of the gospel.

We came in as people who could not save ourselves — not because we had not tried, but because the problem ran deeper than effort could reach. And we encountered a God who, at the cost of his own Son, did what we could not do. He reached across the gap. He paid what we owed. He declared us righteous — not because we had earned it, but because Jesus had.

That is grace. And the only fitting response to a gift this extraordinary is not to try to earn it — it is simply to receive it, with open hands, and to live as those who know they are loved.
