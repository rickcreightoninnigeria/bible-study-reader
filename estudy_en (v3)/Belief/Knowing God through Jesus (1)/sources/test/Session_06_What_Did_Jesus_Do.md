## The Cross and the Resurrection

[type: text; subtype: intro]

Something unexpected happens at the centre of the Christian story. After everything we have learned — about who God is, who Jesus is, and the depth of the problem sin creates — we arrive here: a cross. Not a throne. Not a victory parade. A crucifixion.

For many people, this is the hardest part of the gospel to understand. Why would God allow this? Why would Jesus — Son of God, Lord of creation — die like a criminal? And what could a death two thousand years ago possibly have to do with us today?

This session sits at the very heart of the Christian faith. Everything before it has been preparation. Everything after it flows from what happened here. We will look at two things: what Jesus did on the cross, and what God did three days later. Together, they change everything.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding; eyebrow: "Common Misunderstanding"; term: "Did Jesus really die on the cross?"]

The Quran (Surah 4:157) says that Jesus was not crucified — that it only appeared so to those who saw it. This is worth thinking carefully about.

The crucifixion of Jesus is one of the most historically well-documented events of the ancient world. It is recorded not only in the Gospels but in non-Christian sources — including the Roman historian Tacitus (writing c. AD 116) and the Jewish historian Josephus (c. AD 93). The disciples who proclaimed the resurrection did so at great personal cost — many were imprisoned or killed for this claim. People do not die for what they know to be false.

The Christian claim is not that something happened *to* Jesus against his will — but that he went to the cross *deliberately*, as an act of rescue. It was not defeat. It was the mission.

## Part 2 — The Resurrection

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

If the cross is the heart of what Jesus did, the resurrection is God's verdict on it. Without the resurrection, the cross is just a tragedy. With it, everything changes — for Jesus, and for everyone who trusts in him.

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: "For Reflection"; term: "What the Cross Says About God"]

The cross does not reveal a God who is reluctantly persuaded to forgive. It reveals a God who *initiates* forgiveness at enormous personal cost. The Father sends the Son. The Son gives his life willingly. The Spirit raised him from the dead.

This is not religion as effort — this is grace as gift.

[type: text; subtype: closing]

The cross and the resurrection are not just the centre of Christian theology — they are the centre of Christian life. Everything we do, every day, is lived in the light of this: Jesus gave his life for us, God raised him from the dead, and nothing we face is outside the reach of that love.

You did not earn this. You could not. That is the point. It is a gift — the gift that makes every other gift possible.

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: "Common Questions — Leader Notes"; term: "Anticipated Questions This Session"]

**"The Quran says Jesus was not crucified — someone else was made to look like him."**
Acknowledge this with respect. Then note: the crucifixion is one of the most historically attested events of the ancient world, referenced in Roman and Jewish sources outside the Bible. The disciples — many of whom died for this claim — insisted they had seen the risen Jesus. People do not willingly die for what they know to be a lie.

**"Why would God allow his prophet to die like that?"**
The Christian answer is that this was not something that *happened to* Jesus against his will — it was the very purpose for which he came (Mark 10:45). His death was not defeat; it was the means of rescue. He chose it.

**"How do we know the resurrection really happened?"**
1 Corinthians 15:3–8 records an early creed — scholars date the tradition it preserves to within a few years of the events themselves. Paul lists named witnesses who were still alive. The tomb was empty — a fact his opponents never disputed, only tried to explain away. The disciples were transformed from terrified fugitives into fearless proclaimers who turned the world upside down. Something happened.
