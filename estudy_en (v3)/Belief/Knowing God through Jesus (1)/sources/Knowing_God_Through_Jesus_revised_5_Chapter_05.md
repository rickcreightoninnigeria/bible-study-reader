<!-- CHAPTER INTRODUCTION -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: intro] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: intro]**

Imagine a doctor who tells you your symptoms are minor — that you simply need to rest more and try harder. You follow the advice. But the pain keeps returning, because the doctor missed the real diagnosis. The problem was never your behaviour. It was something deeper that no amount of effort could fix.

Many people who have taken their faith seriously describe exactly this kind of restlessness — a sense that something is unresolved even after sincere religious practice. They prayed faithfully, fasted carefully, tried to obey diligently — and still something felt wrong at a level that effort couldn’t reach. If that is part of your experience, the Bible has a name for it. The problem is not a failure of devotion. It is that the diagnosis was incomplete.

That is what this chapter is for. Many of us arrive with a deeply ingrained instinct: the fundamental problem between us and God is what we *do* — specific sins, particular failures. And the solution, it follows, must be to *do better*: pray more, resist more, discipline ourselves more strictly.

But the Bible tells a different story. The problem is not simply what we do. It is what we *are* — what has gone wrong at the very centre of who we are. Before the gospel can be good news, we need to understand clearly what it is good news *about*.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
Part 2: No One is Exempt

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

If Jesus’ teaching in Mark 7 identifies *where* the problem is — inside the human heart — then Paul’s letter to the Romans identifies *how far* it extends. His argument is sustained and deeply uncomfortable: the problem is not limited to the obviously wicked. It reaches everyone. The religious and the irreligious. The devout and the careless. The well-intentioned and the openly rebellious.

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

Notice that Paul does not end at verse 23. He continues immediately into verse 24: *“But they are justified freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus.”* The diagnosis of sin is not the last word. It is the necessary preparation for the gospel. A doctor must name the illness before the medicine makes sense. Paul names the illness clearly and unflinchingly — precisely because the cure is extraordinary, and it must be received, not earned.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
Part 3: The Separation Sin Creates

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

So far we have seen that sin comes from inside the human heart, and that it extends to all people without exception. But what does sin actually *do*? Is it simply a moral accounting problem — a debt that accumulates and must eventually be settled? The prophet Isaiah goes deeper than that.

Many people describe a religious life in which prayer felt like it was going nowhere — as though God was not listening, as though they were speaking into silence. Isaiah names that experience directly and gives it a theological explanation.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
A Framework: Two Ways of Understanding the Problem

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE / SUMMARY TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

It may help to set two contrasting understandings of sin alongside each other — not to argue, but to see clearly what the Bible is actually saying.

**A common instinct:**
The human being is essentially capable — we know what is right; we simply lose sight of it and need to be reminded. Sin is forgetfulness, drifting, a failure to obey clearly enough. The fix, therefore, is to try harder, do more, discipline yourself more carefully. If the problem is forgetting, then the solution is reminding. A prophet who speaks God’s words and calls us back to him makes perfect sense as the solution. With enough effort and the right guidance, the gap can be closed.

**What the Bible teaches:**
Sin is a condition of the heart — a disordering of who we are at our deepest level. It is not simply that we forget God from time to time; it is that something has gone wrong at the root. We are not just forgetful — we are, as Romans 3 puts it, unable to seek God at all on our own. Because the problem runs that deep, the solution must come from *outside* us entirely. A broken compass cannot be fixed by giving it better directions — it needs to be repaired from the inside. That is why what is needed is not a reminder but a rescue, not a messenger pointing the way, but a saviour who carries us there.

A life of careful obedience — where each act must be correct, where the outcome is never certain until the end — is an exhausting place to live. The Bible does not dismiss that effort. But it offers something different: not a higher standard to reach, but a gift to receive.

This is not meant to discourage. It is meant to liberate. When we see clearly that we cannot save ourselves, we are ready to receive the salvation that God offers as a gift.

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “But surely God rewards those who try hard and are sincere?”
**Question:** If God sees our effort and sincerity, doesn’t that count for something?
**Answer:** Yes — God sees effort, sincerity, and genuine devotion. The Bible never dismisses these things. But Romans 3 is making a different and more fundamental point: that no one, however sincere or devout, *fully meets the standard of God’s perfect holiness*. That gap — between our best and God’s perfection — is precisely the problem the gospel addresses. The issue is not that God fails to notice our sincerity. The issue is that sincerity alone cannot bridge a gap that runs to the very root of our nature. What is needed is not more effort but a new kind of righteousness — one that comes from God himself. That is what Paul introduces in verse 21: “the righteousness of God... through the faithfulness of Jesus Christ.”

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Is sin really that serious? Isn’t God merciful enough to overlook it?”
**Question:** If God is merciful and compassionate, why can’t he simply forgive and move on?
**Answer:** God *is* merciful — extraordinarily, overwhelmingly so. But Isaiah 59:1–2 shows that the issue is not God’s willingness to forgive; it is the reality of what sin *does*. It creates a separation — an alienation — that cannot simply be wished away without something real happening to deal with it. Think of it this way: a judge who overlooks every crime in the name of mercy is not a good judge — he is an unjust one. True mercy and true justice must both be satisfied. This is exactly what the cross achieves: it is the place where God’s justice and his mercy meet. Sin is serious because the separation it causes is real. But the good news is that God himself is the one who bridges it — at great cost to himself.

**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Why does Jesus need to be a saviour? Couldn’t a great prophet be enough?”
**Question:** Many traditions honour Jesus as a great prophet. Why do Christians insist he must be more than that?

If the problem between us and God is mainly that people have forgotten him — that we drift and need to be called back — then a prophet makes perfect sense. The prophet speaks God’s words, calls people back to God, and shows the way. And it is true that human beings do forget God, and that this matters.

A broken compass cannot be fixed by giving it better directions — it needs to be repaired from the inside. That is why the Bible says what is needed is not a reminder but a rescue; not a messenger pointing the way, but a saviour who carries us there.

This is the logic of the cross. Jesus did not come primarily to teach us better — he came to do for us what we could not do for ourselves.

<!-- COMMON QUESTION 5 -->

<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Does this mean all people are equally sinful? What about genuine evil-doers compared to ordinary people?”
**Question:** Surely there are degrees of sin? Paul’s “no distinction” seems too sweeping.
**Answer:** Paul is not saying that all actions are equally harmful or that there are no moral distinctions between people. Clearly some acts are more destructive than others. His point is different: that *in terms of our standing before God’s perfect holiness*, no one has achieved what they were made for. Every human being, however outwardly good, falls short of the glory of God. It is like the difference between someone who swims halfway across a wide ocean and someone who barely leaves the shore — they are genuinely different in ability, but both are unable to reach the other side. The solution for both is the same: they need to be rescued. Paul’s universal diagnosis is not meant to flatten all moral difference; it is meant to close off the escape route that says, “I am good enough not to need what Jesus offers.”

**[type: text; subtype: closing]**

The Bible’s teaching on sin is honest — even brutal in its thoroughness. But it is honest in the way that a good doctor is honest: not to wound, but to heal. The diagnosis of the human heart is not the last word. It is the first word of a story that ends in rescue, in forgiveness, in a righteousness that comes not from our effort but from God’s grace. We have seen the problem clearly in this chapter. In the next, we will see the extraordinary lengths to which God went to solve it — in the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus.
