<!-- [type: text; subtype: intro] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: intro]**

Imagine a doctor who tells you your symptoms are minor — that you simply need to rest more and try harder. You follow the advice. But the pain keeps returning, because the doctor missed the real diagnosis. The problem was never your behaviour. It was something deeper that no amount of effort could fix.

Many people who have taken their faith seriously describe exactly this kind of restlessness — a sense that something is unresolved even after sincere religious practice. They prayed faithfully, fasted carefully, tried to obey diligently — and still something felt wrong at a level that effort couldn't reach. If that is part of your experience, the Bible has a name for it. The problem is not a failure of devotion. It is that the diagnosis was incomplete.

That is what this session is for. Many of us arrive with a deeply ingrained instinct: the fundamental problem between us and God is what we *do* — specific sins, particular failures. And the solution, it follows, must be to *do better*: pray more, resist more, discipline ourselves more strictly.

But the Bible tells a different story. The problem is not simply what we do. It is what we *are* — what has gone wrong at the very centre of who we are. Before the gospel can be good news, we need to understand clearly what it is good news *about*.

So far we have seen that sin comes from inside the human heart, and that it extends to all people without exception. But what does sin actually *do*? Is it simply a moral accounting problem — a debt that accumulates and must eventually be settled? The prophet Isaiah goes deeper than that.

Many people describe a religious life in which prayer felt like it was going nowhere — as though God was not listening, as though they were speaking into silence. Isaiah names that experience directly and gives it a theological explanation.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
A Framework: Two Ways of Understanding the Problem

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE / SUMMARY TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

It may help to set two contrasting understandings of sin alongside each other — not to argue, but to see clearly what the Bible is actually saying.

**A common instinct:**
The human being is essentially capable — we know what is right; we simply lose sight of it and need to be reminded. Sin is forgetfulness, drifting, a failure to obey clearly enough. The fix, therefore, is to try harder, do more, discipline yourself more carefully. A prophet who speaks God's words and calls us back to him makes perfect sense as the solution. With enough effort and the right guidance, the gap can be closed.

**What the Bible teaches:**
Sin is a condition of the heart — a disordering of who we are at our deepest level. It is not simply that we forget God from time to time; it is that something has gone wrong at the root. We are not just forgetful — we are, as Romans 3 puts it, unable to seek God at all on our own. Because the problem runs that deep, the solution must come from *outside* us entirely. A broken compass cannot be fixed by giving it better directions — it needs to be repaired from the inside. That is why what is needed is not a reminder but a rescue, not a messenger pointing the way, but a saviour who carries us there.

A life of careful obedience — where each act must be correct, where the outcome is never certain until the end — is an exhausting place to live. The Bible does not dismiss that effort. But it offers something different: not a higher standard to reach, but a gift to receive.

This is not meant to discourage. It is meant to liberate. When we see clearly that we cannot save ourselves, we are ready to receive the salvation that God offers as a gift.

# Session 6 — What Did Jesus Do?
## The Cross and the Resurrection

---

<!-- ============================================================
     ELEMENT TAGS KEY
     Each section is prefixed with a tag indicating the
     recommended JSON element type/subtype from chapterTemplateV2.
     ============================================================ -->

---

[type: text; subtype: intro]

Something unexpected happens at the centre of the Christian story. After everything we have learned — about who God is, who Jesus is, the depth of what sin does — we arrive here: a cross. Not a throne. Not a victory parade. A crucifixion.

For many people, this is the hardest part of the gospel. Why would God allow this? Why would Jesus — Son of God, Lord of creation — die like a criminal? What could a death two thousand years ago possibly have to do with us today?

This session sits at the very heart of the Christian faith. Everything before it has been preparation. Everything after it flows from what happened here. We will look at two things: what Jesus did on the cross, and what God did three days later. Together, they change everything.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding; eyebrow: "Common Misunderstanding"; term: "Did Jesus really die on the cross?"]

The Quran (Surah 4:157) says that Jesus was not crucified — that it only appeared so to those who saw it. This is worth thinking carefully about.

The crucifixion of Jesus is one of the most historically well-documented events of the ancient world. It is recorded not only in the Gospels but in non-Christian sources — including the Roman historian Tacitus (writing c. AD 116) and the Jewish historian Josephus (c. AD 93). The Christian claim is not that something happened *to* Jesus against his will — but that he went to the cross *deliberately*, as an act of rescue. It was not defeat. It was the mission.

# Session 7 — How Are We Saved? *Grace, Not Works*

> **Key Texts:** Ephesians 2:1–10 · Luke 18:9–14 · Romans 4:4–5
> **Big Idea:** We are saved by grace through faith — not by works. Salvation is a gift to receive, not an achievement to earn.

## Chapter Introduction

[type: text; subtype: intro]

Most of us arrive at faith carrying a deep instinct: *God's acceptance must be earned.* Pray faithfully. Fast sincerely. Obey carefully. Do enough, and perhaps — *perhaps* — the scales will tip in your favour.

It is a deeply human way to think about God. And for those who have come from a background of careful religious practice, it runs even deeper. We have been formed by it. Honoured for it.

But in this session, Jesus and Paul are going to say something that cuts straight across that instinct — something that at first sounds almost too good to be true, and then, slowly, begins to feel like the most liberating thing you have ever heard.

Salvation is not an achievement. It is a gift.

We are not saved because we were good enough. We are saved because God was gracious enough. And that changes everything — including what it means to live well before him.

This is not a claim about your goodness. It is a claim about his faithfulness.

## Big Picture Truths

[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]

**Big Picture Truths**

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Three truths to hold clearly from this session:

**Grace** is not God lowering his standards. It is God, at great cost to himself, meeting us where we could never reach him. The cross shows that sin was taken seriously — completely seriously — and yet forgiven completely.

**Faith** is not a religious work we perform to earn God's favour. It is the open hand that receives a gift. It is trust. It is dependence. It is saying: *I cannot do this. I am relying entirely on what Christ has done.*

**Works** matter — enormously. But they flow from salvation; they do not produce it. Ephesians 2:10 says we are created in Christ Jesus *for* good works. We do not work in order to be accepted. We work *because* we are accepted.

**"Do I need to attend a church? What if it isn't safe for me to do so?"**

Help participants identify the safest and most sustainable form of Christian community available to them.

**"What about my old religious practices — prayer times, fasting, dress — do I have to abandon them all immediately?"**
This is a question of pastoral wisdom more than doctrine. Some practices are neutral forms that can be filled with new Christian content (times of prayer, for example). Others are tied to beliefs that are no longer held. Give space for this to be worked out over time, without pressure. The key question is not "What do I stop doing?" but "Is Jesus Lord of my whole life?"

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding; eyebrow: "Common Misunderstanding"; term: "Witness Means Arguing"]

Many people assume that bearing witness to Jesus means winning arguments — having the right answers to every objection, debating theology, or persuading people through logic alone.

Peter's picture is different. The witness that tends to open hearts is a *life* — marked by genuine goodness, unusual peace, and a hope that is hard to explain. The questions come to you. And when they do, you answer simply, honestly, and with respect.

You are not responsible for changing anyone's heart. That is God's work. You are responsible for living in a way that makes him visible — and being ready to speak when the door opens.

## Part 3 — Handling Pressure and Opposition

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Peter wrote his letter to people facing real opposition — not hypothetical discomfort, but genuine suffering for their faith. He does not tell them to avoid it, minimise it, or pretend it isn't happening. He tells them something stranger and more powerful: that suffering for doing right is not evidence that God has abandoned you. It may be evidence of exactly the opposite.

## Part 4 — The Foundation That Cannot Be Moved

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Everything in this session — witness, gentleness, endurance under pressure — rests on a foundation. And Paul, in Romans 8, gives us that foundation in its most unshakeable form. This is not a passage to study. It is a passage to inhabit.

Read it slowly. Read it as if every line is addressed to you personally. Because it is.

---

[type: biblePassage]

**Bible Reference:** Romans 8:31–39 (NET)
> "What then shall we say about these things? If God is for us, who can be against us? Indeed, he who did not spare his own Son, but gave him up for us all — how will he not also, along with him, freely give us all things? Who will bring any charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is the one who will condemn? Christ Jesus is the one who died, and more than that, was raised, and is at the right hand of God, and intercedes for us. Who will separate us from the love of Christ? Will trouble, or distress, or persecution, or famine, or nakedness, or danger, or sword? As it is written: 'For your sake we encounter death all day long; we are regarded as sheep to be slaughtered.' No, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him who loved us. For I am convinced that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor heavenly rulers, nor things that are present, nor things that are coming, nor powers, nor height, nor depth, nor anything else in creation will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord."

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: "For Reflection"; term: "The Love That Goes First"]

Throughout this series, one truth has appeared again and again from different angles: God's love is not a reward for the right behaviour. It goes *first*. It went first at the cross — "while we were still sinners, Christ died for us." It goes first now — "the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord" is already yours, already surrounding you, already holding you.

You did not begin to love God because you were strong or certain or free from doubt. You began to love him because he first loved you.

That love does not fluctuate with your performance. It does not cool when you fail. It cannot be stripped from you by pressure, opposition, or fear.

This is the ground beneath your feet. It will not shift.

[type: text; subtype: closing]

This is where the series ends — but it is not where your story ends. It is barely where it begins.

You have heard who God is. You have understood what Jesus did. You know that you are saved by grace, that you are a child of God, that you belong to a family that death cannot dissolve. You go now not as someone trying to earn a place at the table, but as someone who already has one.

The road ahead will have hard stretches. There will be questions you cannot answer, pressures you did not expect, and moments when the love of God feels far away. In those moments, return to what you know. Return to the cross. Return to the empty tomb. Return to Romans 8.

And remember: the same God who did not spare his own Son for you will not abandon you now.

Go in confidence. Go in gentleness. Go as light.

As this series closes, consider three things to carry with you:

**One truth to anchor you:** Choose a verse from this series — perhaps Romans 8:38–39, or John 15:9, or Romans 5:8 — and commit it to memory. Let it be the thing you return to when pressure comes.

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: "Common Questions — Leader Notes"; term: "Anticipated Questions This Session"]

**"What if my family finds out and things get much worse?"**
Do not minimise this fear — it is real and the consequences can be severe. Walk through 1 Peter 3:13–16 together and affirm that Jesus does not ask for recklessness. Wisdom about timing and context is not cowardice. At the same time, gently affirm that living in sustained secrecy has its own cost — spiritually and emotionally. Pray together specifically for wisdom about how and when to speak. If practical safety needs exist, address them concretely.

**"I don't feel confident enough to tell anyone about my faith — I still have too many doubts."**
This is worth addressing warmly. Peter does not say to be ready to give a *complete theological account* — he says to be ready to explain "the hope you possess." You do not need to have resolved every question to speak about what has changed in you. Doubt and witness can coexist. In fact, honest witness from a place of wrestling is often more compelling than polished certainty.

**"What about my Muslim family and friends — are they condemned?"**
Handle this with great care and tenderness. Avoid giving a cold doctrinal answer to what is a deeply personal question. Acknowledge that this is one of the hardest parts of the Christian journey for people in this group. Affirm what is true: God is the judge, not us. Our calling is to love, to pray, and to live in a way that makes Jesus visible. Direct participants toward Romans 9–11 for Paul's own wrestling with this for his own people.

**"Is this the end — what happens now?"**
This is an important pastoral moment. Be clear about what ongoing support, community, and discipleship looks like beyond this series. If there is a church community, small group, or one-to-one mentoring available, name it concretely. The transition out of a structured series can feel destabilising. Make sure no one leaves without a clear next step and a known person to contact.
