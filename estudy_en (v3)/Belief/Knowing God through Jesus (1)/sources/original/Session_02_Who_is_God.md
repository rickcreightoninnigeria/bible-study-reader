[type: text; subtype: intro]

Every person has a picture of God — even those who say they do not. That picture shapes everything: how we pray, how we feel when we fail, whether we draw near or keep our distance, whether we expect love or only judgement.

For many people, that picture is of a God who is vast, sovereign, and powerful — a King, a Judge, a Creator. And those things are absolutely true. But the Bible claims something more: that this same God, the maker of heaven and earth, is also *personal*. That he can be known, not just feared. That he draws near, not just commands from a distance.

This session is not about replacing what you already know of God's greatness. It is about expanding it — because the God revealed in Jesus is greater *and* closer than any picture we could have formed on our own.

[type: heading; subtype: section]
**TEXT:** Part 1 — The God Who Is Merciful and Near

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Psalm 103 is one of the most beloved poems in all of Scripture. It was written by David — a man who had known both the heights of God's blessing and the depths of his own failure — and it is a sustained meditation on the character of God. What does God actually *like*? What are his instincts toward human beings?

The answer, from beginning to end, is: compassion.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**EYEBROW:** Common Question
**TERM:** "Is the God of the Bible the same as Allah?"
**QUESTION:** You may have wondered: are Christians and Muslims talking about the same God? Think about it before tapping to read a response.
**ANSWER:** This is a question worth taking seriously rather than brushing aside. There is one Creator God — the God of Abraham, who made the heavens and the earth. In that sense, yes: Christians and Muslims are not worshipping different beings.

But the Bible makes claims about this God that go further than other traditions. It claims that God is not only Creator and King, but that he is personally relational by nature — that within the one God there is a relationship of Father, Son, and Spirit (we will explore this more in Sessions 3 and 4). And it claims that this God has acted decisively in history by coming in the person of Jesus.

The honest answer, then, is: *same God* — but a much fuller and more surprising revelation of who that God is. This series exists to help you explore that revelation.

[type: heading; subtype: section]
**TEXT:** Part 2 — The Word Who Became Flesh

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Psalm 103 tells us a great deal about what God is *like*. But John's Gospel goes even further: it tells us that God has made himself known in a person.

The opening of John's Gospel — sometimes called the Prologue — is one of the most theologically dense passages in all of Scripture. It describes Jesus as the *Logos* — the Word — and makes a staggering claim: that this Word is not a created being, but is God himself, and has entered the world in human form.

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

John 1 introduces us to something we will return to in Session 4 — the mystery of the Trinity. But even before we try to understand the *structure* of that mystery, we can notice what it means for us: God is not a distant, unknowable sovereign. He is the kind of God who enters the world, who "tabernacles" among his people, who can be seen and touched and heard.

The question "Can we know God personally?" is answered in Jesus: yes. Because Jesus is God making himself personally known.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — Calling God "Father"

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Of all the things Jesus taught his disciples, one of the most unexpected was how to address God in prayer. He did not say: begin with God's sovereignty. He did not say: establish your unworthiness first. He said: begin with *Father*.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**EYEBROW:** Common Question
**TERM:** Calling God "Father" — isn't that disrespectful?
**QUESTION:** To some, addressing God as "Father" feels presumptuous, or even disrespectful. What is your instinct when you hear this language? Think about it before tapping to read a response.
**ANSWER:** This reaction is understandable, and worth taking seriously. In some cultures, calling God "Father" sounds like a reduction of his greatness — as if we are making him too ordinary, too familiar.

But Jesus' intention is the opposite. The Aramaic word he would have used — *Abba* — is a word of deep trust and intimacy, used by a child who knows they are loved and safe. It is not casual disrespect; it is the language of secure belonging.

Crucially, this is not a title we invented for God — it is the title *Jesus himself* uses, and it is the title he invites *us* to use. To call God Father is to accept the relationship he has offered, not to claim an equality we have earned.

In saying "Our Father," Jesus is not making a biological claim. He is revealing something about God's character: that the God who made the universe *wants* his people near. He is not a distant king who tolerates subjects — he is a Father who welcomes children.

[type: heading; subtype: section]
**TEXT:** Part 4 — God's Greatness and God's Nearness Together

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

It is worth pausing to hold two things together that might feel in tension: God is infinitely great — Creator, King, Judge — *and* he is intimately personal, near, and knowable. The Bible does not ask us to choose between these. In fact, it insists on both.

Psalm 103 celebrates both. John 1 holds both. The Lord's Prayer begins with the hallowing of God's name — his holiness and greatness — and in the very same breath calls him *Father*.

The most remarkable claim in this session is not that God is powerful. It is that the powerful God *wants to be known by you*.

[type: heading; subtype: section]
**TEXT:** Big Picture Truths

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

Here are the three key truths from this session to hold on to:

**1. God is both great and near.** His sovereignty, power, and holiness are never in question. But the Bible adds something: this same God draws near to those he loves. Greatness and intimacy are not in conflict — in God, they are inseparable.

**2. Jesus is the fullest revelation of God.** All that God is — his character, his love, his purposes — is made visible in Jesus. To look at Jesus is to see what God is like. John puts it plainly: "The only one, himself God... has made God known."

**3. God invites us to call him Father.** This is not a human invention. It is the invitation of Jesus himself. To accept it is not presumption — it is faith.

[type: text; subtype: closing]

The God you have come to through Jesus is not a distant, unknowable force. He is not a God you must constantly appease, wondering if you have done enough. He is the God who, according to Psalm 103, knows you are made of clay — and loves you anyway. He is the God who, according to John 1, entered the world in person so that you might know him. He is the God who, according to Jesus, wants you to call him Father.

That is not a small thing. It may take time to feel true. But it *is* true — and we will keep returning to it throughout this series.
