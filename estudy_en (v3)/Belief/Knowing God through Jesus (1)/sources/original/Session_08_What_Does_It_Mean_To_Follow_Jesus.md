[type: text; subtype: intro]

By now, something significant has happened. You have heard who Jesus is. You have understood what he did on the cross and why his resurrection changes everything. You have seen that salvation is not something you earn — it is a gift you receive.

But a gift, once received, changes you. And a relationship, once begun, has to be lived.

This session turns to a practical question: *What does it actually look like to follow Jesus?* Not in an ideal world, or for someone with a different family, a different culture, a different set of pressures — but for you, here, in the life you are already living.

Jesus is honest about this. Following him is not pain-free. But it is also not a crushing set of new rules to keep. It is an invitation into something that — over time — begins to feel less like a burden and more like rest.

## Part 1 — The Cost Is Real

## Part 2 — The Relationship That Sustains It

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

In John 15, Jesus gives his disciples one of his most vivid pictures of what following him looks like from the inside. It is not primarily a picture of effort — it is a picture of connection. The question is not "How hard are you trying?" but "Are you staying close?"

---

[type: biblePassage]

**Bible Reference:** John 15:1–11 (NET)
> "I am the true vine and my Father is the gardener. He takes away every branch that does not bear fruit in me. He prunes every branch that bears fruit so that it will bear more fruit. You are clean already because of the word that I have spoken to you. Remain in me, and I will remain in you. Just as the branch cannot bear fruit by itself, unless it remains in the vine, so neither can you unless you remain in me. I am the vine; you are the branches. The one who remains in me — and I in him — bears much fruit, because apart from me you can accomplish nothing. If anyone does not remain in me, he is thrown out like a branch and dries up; and such branches are gathered up and thrown into the fire, and they are burned up. If you remain in me and my words remain in you, ask whatever you want, and it will be done for you. My Father is glorified by this, that you bear much fruit and prove to be my disciples. Just as the Father has loved me, I have also loved you; remain in my love. If you obey my commandments, you will remain in my love, just as I have obeyed my Father's commandments and remain in his love. I have told you these things so that my joy may be in you, and your joy may be complete."

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding; eyebrow: "Common Misunderstanding"; term: "Obedience and Fear"]

Many people — especially those who have come from a religious background that emphasised duty and divine judgment — carry a picture of obedience rooted in fear. *Obey, or face consequences.*

Jesus offers a different picture. In John 15:9–10, obedience flows from love, not fear: *"Just as the Father has loved me, I have also loved you; remain in my love. If you obey my commandments, you will remain in my love."*

The order matters: love comes first, then obedience. You do not obey to win Jesus' love. You obey *because* you already have it — and because you want to stay close to the one who loves you.

This is not a minor adjustment. It reshapes the entire feel of the Christian life.

*Does this feel different from how you have thought about obedience in the past?*

## Part 3 — A Different Kind of Yoke

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

In Jesus' day, a "yoke" was a wooden frame placed over oxen to help them plough. A rabbi's "yoke" meant his teaching — the weight of obligations his students took on by following him. Some yokes were heavy. Some broke people.

Jesus offers a different one.

---

[type: biblePassage]

**Bible Reference:** Matthew 11:28–30 (NET)
> "Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke on you and learn from me, because I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy to bear, and my load is not heavy."

## Part 4 — What Discipleship Looks Like Day to Day

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: "For Reflection"; term: "The Long Game"]

Discipleship is not a sprint. It is a lifetime of small choices — to stay close, to trust, to obey, to return when you wander. The Christian life is not about achieving a level of holiness before God will be pleased with you. It is about a relationship that is already secure, being worked out in daily life.

The branch does not strain to produce fruit. It stays connected to the vine — and fruit comes naturally from that connection, over time.

Be patient with yourself. Be patient with the process. Jesus is.

[type: text; subtype: closing]

Following Jesus is the most demanding and the most freeing thing a person can do. It is demanding because it asks for everything — a whole self, not just a set of religious observances. It is freeing because the one asking is not a distant judge keeping score, but a shepherd who knows your name, a vine that gives you life, a teacher whose yoke turns out to be rest.

You are not following a set of rules. You are following a person. And that person has already proven — on a cross and through an empty tomb — that there is nowhere he will not go for you.

Next session we will explore what it means to receive the new identity that this relationship creates: not just a follower of Jesus, but a *child of God*, with all the belonging that entails.

[type: callout; subtype: general; eyebrow: "Common Questions — Leader Notes"; term: "Anticipated Questions This Session"]

**"Can I really follow Jesus if my family doesn't know yet — or if I have to hide it?"**
Be honest and pastoral here. Many in the group will be in exactly this situation. Discipleship does not require immediate public declaration in every context — wisdom and timing matter. But it does require an honest orientation of the heart toward Jesus, and a growing desire to live for him even in hidden ways. The question of witness will be addressed more fully in Session 10.

**"What if I fail? What if I sin and fall back into old patterns?"**
This is one of the most important questions to answer well. Discipleship is not about a perfect record — it is about the direction of travel. The branch that is still connected to the vine is still alive, even when it looks scraggly. Returning to Jesus after failure is itself an act of discipleship. 1 John 1:9 is a helpful anchor: "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and righteous, forgiving us our sins and cleansing us from all unrighteousness."

**"Do I need to attend a church? What if it isn't safe for me to do so?"**
The community dimension of discipleship is real and important — we are not designed to follow Jesus alone. But "church" may need to look different in different contexts: a small trusted group, an online community, regular meetings with one or two other believers. What matters is that you are not isolated. Help participants identify the safest and most sustainable form of Christian community available to them.

**"What about my old religious practices — prayer times, fasting, dress — do I have to abandon them all immediately?"**
This is a question of pastoral wisdom more than doctrine. Some practices are neutral forms that can be filled with new Christian content (times of prayer, for example). Others are tied to beliefs that are no longer held. Give space for this to be worked out over time, without pressure. The key question is not "What do I stop doing?" but "Is Jesus Lord of my whole life?"
