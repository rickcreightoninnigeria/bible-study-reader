<!-- CHAPTER INTRODUCTION -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: intro] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: intro]**

In the previous chapter we saw who Jesus is through what he *does and says* — forgiving sin, commanding nature, bearing the title that belongs to God alone. Now we go deeper into a question that has puzzled, challenged, and astonished people ever since: *how can Jesus be fully God, if God is one?*

The honest answer is: this is a mystery. No one has resolved it in a single sitting, and we won’t either. But we can do something more useful: we can let Jesus himself speak. The words we will read come from the upper room on the night before the crucifixion — the most intimate teaching Jesus ever gave his disciples. In those hours, he spoke with unusual clarity about his relationship with the Father, and about the Spirit he was about to send.

The doctrine we call the Trinity is not a formula invented by church councils. It is rooted in what Jesus said and did. So we begin where we should always begin: with Scripture.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
One God — Not Three

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

Before we read the passages, it helps to clear away a common misunderstanding. The Christian teaching about the Trinity is frequently misunderstood — even by Christians. So let us state clearly what it is *not*, and what it *is*.

Christians do not believe in three gods. That is called tritheism, and the church has always rejected it. Christians believe that there is <b>one God</b> — the same one God affirmed in the Hebrew Scriptures: *“Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one”* (Deuteronomy 6:4). What the New Testament reveals — and what Jesus himself discloses — is that within this one God there is an eternal relationship: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Three distinct persons, one God.

This is not something Christians invented to explain a puzzle. It is something that *forced itself* upon the earliest followers of Jesus because of what they experienced: they were monotheists to their core, and yet they found themselves worshipping Jesus alongside the Father, and experiencing the Spirit as a living, divine presence. The Trinity is the only framework that does justice to all of that.

**[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Misunderstanding
**Term:** “Doesn’t the Quran say the Trinity is Father, Son, and Mary?”
**Question:** The Quran’s objection to the Trinity in Al-Ma’idah 5:116 seems to be directed at a triad of Father, Son, and Mary — not Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. How do Christians respond to that?
**Answer:** Christians respond by saying: we reject that triad too. The Bible nowhere teaches that Mary is part of the Godhead, and no mainstream Christian tradition has ever believed it. The Quran appears to be responding to a real confusion — in fact, a heresy — that existed among some Christians in 7th-century Arabia, where certain groups did venerate Mary in ways that went far beyond what Scripture taught — and may genuinely have appeared to elevate her to a divine status.

The Christian teaching is Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — not Father, Son, and Mary. On that specific point, the Quran’s objection and the Bible’s teaching are in agreement: Mary is a remarkable and honoured woman, but she is not God, and she is not part of the Trinity.

Knowing this can help clear the ground. When Muslims and Christians talk about the Trinity, they are not always talking about the same thing — and it helps to be clear about what Christians actually claim before engaging the question.

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

Jesus’ words here are stunning in their directness. He does not say, “I will point you to the Father,” or “I will tell you about the Father.” He says: *“The person who has seen me has seen the Father.”* This is not a claim that Jesus and the Father are identical — he speaks of the Father as distinct from himself throughout these chapters. But it is a claim that Jesus is the <b>full and final revelation</b> of who God is. To know Jesus is to know God. No messenger or prophet has ever said anything like this — because none could.

**[type: heading; subtype: section; level: 2]**
A Simple Framework for the Trinity

---

<!-- ============================================================ -->
<!-- BRIDGE / SUMMARY TEXT -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

The Trinity is a mystery — but it is a <em>revealed</em> mystery, not an invented one. Here are three things that Christians can say clearly:

**1. There is one God.** Christians are not polytheists. The God of the Old Testament and the God of the New Testament are the same God. He alone is Creator, Lord, and Saviour.

**2. Within that one God, there is an eternal relationship.** Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are genuinely distinct — the Father is not the Son, the Son is not the Spirit — but they are not three separate gods. They are one in being, one in will, and one in love.

**3. We know this because Jesus himself revealed it.** The Trinity is not the result of philosophical speculation. It is the result of listening carefully to what Jesus said and did. He spoke of the Father as distinct from himself. He promised to send the Spirit. He shared glory with the Father before creation. The Trinitarian shape of God is shown in Scripture — not imposed upon it.

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Isn’t the Trinity just *shirk* — associating partners with God?”
**Question:** This is the most important question in this chapter. What do Christians actually mean when they talk about the Trinity — and what are they *not* saying?
**Answer:** This question deserves a careful and respectful answer. *Shirk* — associating created beings or rivals with God — is indeed a grave error, and Christians agree with that completely. But the Trinity is not a claim that God has partners or competitors. It is a claim that the one God eternally exists in a relationship of Father, Son, and Spirit. Jesus is not a second god added to the first; he is the eternal Son *within* the being of the one God. Christians do not worship a created being alongside God — they worship the God who has revealed himself in this Trinitarian way. The concern behind the question is right and good. The Christian answer is that the Trinity, properly understood, is *not* shirk — it is the revealed nature of the one God.

<!-- COMMON QUESTION 2 -->
<!-- [type: callout; subtype: general] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “Was the Trinity invented at the Council of Nicaea in AD 325?”
**Question:** Many people believe the Trinity was a later addition to Christianity. Is that true?
**Answer:** The Council of Nicaea (AD 325) did not *invent* the Trinity — it *defended* it against those who wanted to subtract from it. The council was responding to a teacher called Arius who argued that the Son was a created being, subordinate to the Father. Nicaea said: no — the Son is of the same nature as the Father, just as Scripture teaches. The Trinitarian structure itself is found in the very earliest New Testament documents: 2 Corinthians 13:14 was written around AD 55, less than 25 years after the crucifixion. Jesus himself speaks in Trinitarian patterns throughout John 14–17. Nicaea clarified and defended what was already there. It did not create it.

<!-- COMMON QUESTION 3 -->
<!-- [type: callout; subtype: general] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: callout; subtype: general]**
**Eyebrow:** Common Question
**Term:** “How can God have a Son? Doesn’t that imply something physical?”
**Question:** The phrase “Son of God” can sound biological or physical. What does it actually mean?
**Answer:** This is an understandable concern, and it is important to answer it clearly. “Son of God” does not mean that God had a child through any physical process. It is not that kind of language.

Arabic actually has two different words that are relevant and helpful here. *Walad* refers to a biological offspring — a child born through physical means. *Ibn* refers to a son in the relational, originating sense — one who comes from another, bears their nature, and stands in close relationship. The Quran’s objection to the idea of God having a son is specifically to the *walad* — and Christians agree completely: that is not what is meant. The Bible’s claim is of the *ibn* kind: Jesus is the one who originates from the very being of God, shares his nature, and stands in eternal relationship with the Father.

The Nicene Creed, written in the fourth century to address exactly this question, puts it this way: Jesus is *“begotten, not made.”* That phrase is worth reflecting on. *Begotten* means he comes from the Father — he shares the Father’s nature. *Not made* means he is not a creation, not something that came into existence at a point in time. He has always existed in relationship with the Father. “Son of God” names that relationship — the one who eternally proceeds from God’s own being, not the one born through any physical act.

There is something else worth noticing. The Quran itself calls Jesus *kalimatuhu* — “his Word” (An-Nisa 4:171). The Bible explains what that means: not a word written in a book, but the living Word who proceeded from God’s very being and entered the world as a person. “Son of God” and “Word of God” are two ways of pointing at the same truth — that Jesus comes from God, shares God’s nature, and is not a created being like any other.

<!-- BRIDGE TEXT — eternal love and the Trinity -->
<!-- [type: text; subtype: bridge] -->
<!-- ============================================================ -->

**[type: text; subtype: bridge]**

Before we move to reflection, here is one reason why the Trinity is not just a puzzle to wrestle with — but a comfort to rest in.

The Bible says that God is love (1 John 4:8). But love, by its nature, requires someone to love. If God were a single, solitary person before creation existed, he could not have been love — he would have needed the world in order to have someone to love. That would mean God *needed* us to be fully himself.

The Trinity means something entirely different: God’s love did not begin when he made the world. It was already flowing, from eternity, between Father, Son, and Spirit — a relationship of self-giving love that has always existed. God did not create us because he needed love. He created us because he *is* love, and love overflows. Love is not an afterthought, relationships are not an afterthought. Relationships are central to who God is, and to all of reality. Love is central to who God is, and to all of reality.

This is also why Jesus could pray in John 17:24: *“You loved me before the creation of the world.”* The love that reached you is not new. It is eternal — and you have been welcomed into it.

**[type: text; subtype: closing]**

The God revealed in Jesus is not a distant, unknowable force — and he is not a simple, solitary being locked away in transcendence. He is a God of relationship, of love, of eternal self-giving. Before the world existed, Father, Son, and Spirit were already in a communion of love. And the extraordinary news of the gospel is that you have been invited into that communion. Not as an outsider. Not as a servant who must earn entry. But as one whom the Father calls his own, in the Son, by the Spirit. That is the Trinity — not a riddle, but a relationship, opened wide for you.
