Suggested Changes to Session 5 based on Moser
1. Rename or reframe the "fall vs. slip" distinction in the Framework section (Medium-high impact)
The article's most useful contribution for this session is its precise articulation of why the Muslim instinct leads naturally to a "prophet" solution rather than a "saviour" solution — because if sin is forgetfulness (ghafla), a reminder is the logical fix. If sin is a root corruption, only rescue can suffice.
The study's current Framework section ("A common instinct / What the Bible teaches") captures the contrast but doesn't quite name why the two diagnoses lead to such different solutions. A short addition — something like: "If the problem is simply that we forget God from time to time, what we need is a reminder. But if the problem is that something has gone wrong at the root — that we are not just forgetful but broken — then a reminder, however good, cannot reach far enough. What we need is not a messenger, but a rescuer" — would sharpen the session considerably without sounding academic.

2. Add a new Common Question about prophets vs. saviours (High impact)
The study currently has no question that addresses the most natural objection from this audience: "Why does Jesus need to be a saviour? Why isn't a prophet enough?" The Moser article explains exactly why this question arises — it flows logically from a different understanding of what sin is — and that logic can be surfaced accessibly.
A new Common Question card along these lines would strengthen the session:

"Why does Jesus need to be a saviour? Couldn't a great prophet be enough?"
The answer to this depends entirely on what you think the problem is. If the problem between us and God is mainly that people have forgotten him — that we drift and need to be called back — then a prophet makes perfect sense. The prophet speaks God's words, calls people back to God, and shows the way. And Islam is right that human beings do forget God, and that this matters.
But the Bible's diagnosis goes deeper. It is not just that we forget God — it is that something has gone wrong at the root of who we are. We are not just forgetful; we are, as Romans 3 puts it, unable to seek God at all on our own. A broken compass cannot be fixed by giving it better directions. It needs to be repaired from the inside. That is why the Bible says what is needed is not a reminder but a rescue — not a messenger pointing the way, but a saviour who carries us there.


3. Enrich the opening illustration (Low-medium impact)
The doctor illustration at the start is good but generic. The Moser article's "forgetfulness vs. depravity" framing could give it a sharper edge, specifically relevant to this audience. A brief addition after the current opening could acknowledge honestly: "Some people would say the problem is more like forgetting — we know what is right, we just lose sight of it and need to be reminded. The Bible takes that seriously. But it says the problem runs deeper still." This validates the instinct before reframing it, which is more effective pastorally than simply asserting the deeper view.

4. Add a line to the Application Challenge about prophets and what they can and cannot do (Low impact)
The current challenge sends readers to Psalm 51. A single sentence addition could plant a seed: "Notice that David does not ask God to send him a better teacher or remind him more firmly. He asks God to 'create in me a clean heart' — to do something only God himself can do." This builds the prophet/saviour distinction from within the Psalm without making it heavy-handed.

Suggestions for improvement to Sessions 1-4 based on Madrigal
Sessions 3 & 4 — Who is Jesus? / The Trinity

Suggestion 1 — Replace "Son of God" explanation with the Ibnu'llah / Waladu'llah distinction
Currently: Session 3's callout on "Son of God" explains that the title is relational, not biological. Session 4's callout does the same.
What Madrigal adds: He makes a distinction between Ibnu'llah (spiritual son of, originating from God) and Waladu'llah (blood/biological son of God). The Quran's objection is specifically to the walad (biological) concept — and Christians agree that's not what is meant. The title Ibnu'llah as used in the Bible means "the one who originates from the very being of God." This distinction, already familiar to many from a Muslim background, can defuse the objection more precisely than a general denial.
Suggested addition: A sentence or two in the existing callout noting that Arabic actually has two different words here — and that the Bible's claim is the ibnu kind, which even the Quran uses approvingly when it calls Jesus kalimatuhu ("his word"). You don't need to go into Arabic linguistics; just name the distinction plainly.
Tone note: Keep it simple — one short paragraph at most. The goal is recognition, not a lecture.

Suggestion 2 — Add a callout in Session 4 addressing the historical misunderstanding (Father, Son, and Mary)
Currently: Session 4 explains that the Trinity is not three gods, and mentions Nicaea. It doesn't address why the Trinity was misunderstood as three gods in the first place.
What Madrigal adds: He explains that early 7th-century Christianity in Arabia was often theologically confused, and that some Christians were perceived (or actually did) treat the Trinity as Father, Son, and Mary — which is why the Quran (Al-Ma'idah 5:116) frames its objection that way. The Quran condemns that triad — and so do Christians. This is genuinely disarming: it shows that Christians reject the misunderstanding the Quran is responding to.
Suggested addition: A short callout; subtype: misunderstanding in Session 4, something like: "The Quran's objection to the Trinity is partly aimed at a version of it that Christians also reject — a triad of Father, Son, and Mary. That was a real confusion in early 7th-century Arabia. The Bible never taught that triad. The Christian claim is Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — and the 'Son' is not a physical offspring but the eternal Word of God."
Why this helps: It shows you've taken the Quran's concern seriously enough to actually read it, rather than dismissing it. That earns trust.

Suggestion 3 — Add the Kalam/Logos bridge to the "Son of God" response in Session 4
Currently: The callout on "How can God have a Son?" explains the title is theological, not biological. It's good, but it relies entirely on Christian framing.
What Madrigal adds: He builds a bridge using kalam — a concept Islamic theology already takes seriously. Islamic scholars affirmed that the Word of God (kalam nafsi, the essential word before it becomes letters and sounds) is eternal and is not a created thing. The Bible says the same: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was God." Madrigal suggests using the Quran's own title for Jesus — kalimatuhu ("his Word," An-Nisa 4:171) — as a bridge. Jesus is called his Word in the Quran; the Bible explains what that means: the eternal, living, self-aware Word of God, who took on human form.
Suggested addition: A sentence in the callout noting that the Quran itself calls Jesus kalimatuhu — "his Word" — and that the Bible explains what that means: not a word sent in a book, but the living Word who proceeded from God's very being and entered the world as a person. "Son of God" and "Word of God" are two ways of pointing at the same truth.
Tone note: This is a bridge, not an argument. Frame it as "here is something you may already recognise, that points toward what we mean."

Suggestion 4 — Add the "eternal love requires the Trinity" insight to Session 4
Currently: Session 4's closing text says God is "a God of relationship, of love, of eternal self-giving" and that "before the world existed, Father, Son, and Spirit were already in a communion of love." This is good, but it's stated rather than shown.
What Madrigal adds: He makes the argument explicit and philosophical, but in a way that can be made accessible: God is love (1 John 4:8). But love requires a lover and a beloved. If God were a single solitary person before creation, he could not have been love — he would have needed creation to express love. The Trinity means God's love is not dependent on creation; it was already flowing eternally between Father, Son, and Spirit. This also explains John 17:24 — "you loved me before the creation of the world."
Suggested addition: A short bridge paragraph in Part 4 of Session 4, before the reflection questions. Something like: "Here is one reason why the Trinity is not just a puzzle but a comfort: it means God's love did not begin when he made the world. He did not need us in order to have someone to love. The love between Father, Son, and Spirit has been flowing from eternity — and it is into that love that you have been welcomed."
Why this helps: It gives people a reason to want the Trinity to be true, not just a reason to accept it intellectually.

Session 3 — Son of God and Lord
Suggestion 5 — Soften or revisit the C. S. Lewis "Lord, Liar, or Lunatic" trilemma
Currently: The study uses Lewis's trilemma as a question. Madrigal doesn't address this directly, but his overall approach is relevant here.
What Madrigal's approach suggests: Madrigal consistently builds understanding from within the participant's own framework rather than presenting Christianity as the only logical option from the outside. The Lewis trilemma, while intellectually useful for Western audiences, can feel adversarial to someone from a Muslim background — it essentially says "your position (great prophet) is logically impossible." Madrigal's approach would be: show what Jesus did and said, let participants sit with it, and let the conclusion form naturally rather than forcing it through a logical argument.
Suggested change: Consider replacing the Lewis trilemma question with something like: "Jesus' actions in these passages go beyond anything a prophet would claim. How do you account for them?" This achieves the same logical work but feels like an invitation to think rather than a trap.

Sessions 1 & 2 — Bible reliability and the character of God
Suggestion 6 — Strengthen the Bible corruption callout with Madrigal's internal Quran argument
Currently: Session 1's callout on "Hasn't the Bible been corrupted?" makes the manuscript evidence argument and notes that Jesus quoted the OT as reliable.
What Madrigal adds: He points out that the Quran itself presents Allah as the direct protector of all holy scriptures (a point he makes without a specific reference, citing the Quran's self-description). The claim that the Bible was corrupted is therefore not just historically implausible — it creates a theological problem within Islam: it implies God failed to protect what he said he would protect.
Suggested addition: One short sentence at the end of the existing callout answer: "There is also a difficulty within the Islamic tradition itself: the Quran speaks of God as the protector of the scriptures he has given. If the Bible was corrupted, the question becomes: did God allow what he said he would protect to be altered?"
Tone note: This should be offered gently as a question to sit with, not as a counter-attack. The goal is to create space for reflection, not to win a debate.

Session 2 — Who is God?
Suggestion 7 — Add the distinction between God's substance and his identity/character to bridge the "same God" question
Currently: Session 2's callout on "Is the God of the Bible the same as Allah?" answers with "same God, but fuller revelation."
What Madrigal adds: He distinguishes between God's substance (wujud — his transcendent being, omnipotence, etc.) and his identity/character (dhat — his personal virtues: love, mercy, holiness). He observes that both Islam and Christianity agree on the former; the difference is that the Bible reveals God's character in a way that goes beyond what can be known from his works alone. This is a more nuanced and respectful framing than "same God, but fuller."
Suggested addition: In the callout answer, add a sentence: "The God of the Bible and the God of the Quran are agreed on what theologians call God's substance — he is eternal, omnipotent, creator, without beginning. Where the Bible goes further is in revealing his personal character: not just that he is powerful, but that he is love at his very core — and that this love was active before creation, within the relationship of Father, Son, and Spirit."

Suggested Improvements Based on Chapman
Session 7 — Grace, Not Works
Suggestion 1: Deepen the explanation of why assurance feels arrogant (Section 5)
The book contains a clear and precise contrast (pp. 270–271) between the Islamic view of the Day of Judgment — where good and bad deeds are literally weighed on a balance, and even then most Muslims can only hope the Prophet's intercession will tip the scales — and the Christian view of present assurance based on what Christ has done. This is richer and more specific than the current callout, which uses the phrase "only God knows." You could add a sentence or two to the callout answer explaining why uncertainty is structurally built into the Islamic view (the balance of deeds, the unknown verdict) — which makes the contrast with Christian assurance sharper and more compassionate. Not to score a debating point, but to say: I understand why certainty sounds strange to you, because the framework you came from deliberately withholds it.
Suggestion 2: Acknowledge the role of Muhammad's intercession in the assurance question
Chapman notes that many Muslims trust in the Prophet's intercession (shafa'a) on the Day of Judgment as a source of hope. The current study doesn't mention this. A brief, respectful acknowledgement in the assurance section — something like: "Some of you may have hoped that the Prophet would intercede for you on that day. The Christian claim is not that such hope is wrong to have, but that Jesus is himself both Judge and Saviour — and that his intercession is not a hope but a certainty, secured by what he has already done" — would resonate with the actual frame of reference participants bring.
Suggestion 3: The intro framing of "earning acceptance" could be more concrete
The book describes the Islamic framework as fundamentally legal and works-based — not just as a cultural feeling but as a structural feature of Islamic theology: law (shari'a) is the very essence of Islam, and relationship with God is mediated through compliance with it. You don't need to say this academically in the intro, but the opening could go one notch further by naming what participants will have concretely done to earn acceptance: the five pillars, the daily prayers, the fasts. Something like "You prayed five times a day. You fasted through Ramadan. You gave to those in need. Each of these was an act of devotion — and each of them was also, somewhere in the back of your mind, part of the account you were hoping would balance in your favour." This lands with more specificity than "praying faithfully."

Session 9 — New Identity, New Family
Suggestion 4: Name the ummah explicitly in the intro or bridge text
Chapman is clear that leaving Islam is experienced not just as a change of religion but as leaving the ummah — the worldwide Muslim community that is simultaneously family, culture, political body, and people. This is why the loss feels so total. The current intro is well-written and pastoral, but it says "family" and "community" in a general way. A single sentence naming what participants have left — "You have not just changed your beliefs. In the eyes of many who love you, you have left the ummah — the community that is your people, your identity, your world" — would validate the loss with a precision that participants will recognise and feel seen by.
Suggestion 5: Add a Common Question about apostasy (ridda)
The book devotes an entire chapter (Chapter 30) to the Islamic teaching on apostasy — and makes the important point that the death penalty is not in the Qur'an itself (which speaks only of divine judgment in the afterlife for apostasy), but comes from hadith and was later historically conflated with political treason. Many participants will be living with the awareness — spoken or unspoken — that their community regards them as apostates. Session 9 has no callout that names this directly. A carefully worded Common Question — "Does Islam teach that I am an apostate, and what does that mean?" — with a compassionate, accurate answer that (a) explains the distinction between Qur'anic and hadith-based teaching, (b) acknowledges the real danger some participants face, and (c) points to the God who calls them beloved — would be pastorally significant. It doesn't have to be long. But its absence is a gap.
Suggestion 6: Strengthen the bridge text on what the new family actually provides that the ummah did
Chapman identifies what the ummah gives its members: belonging, identity, a shared moral framework, community support, a sense of divine purpose. The current bridge text (the "what has been lost and given" section) names the emotional cost well, but doesn't quite articulate what specifically the church offers that meets the same deep needs. You could add a sentence or two noting that the church offers the same things — belonging, identity, shared life, moral community, a sense of being part of something larger — but on different grounds: not birth and blood and law, but grace and adoption and the Spirit.

Across multiple sessions
Suggestion 7: The phrase "from a Muslim background" is used throughout — consider whether "from Islam" is also needed sometimes
Chapman is careful throughout to distinguish between Islam as a religion/system and Muslims as people. The study does this well. One small addition worth considering: in the Common Questions where participants might feel they are being spoken about rather than to, shifting occasionally from third person ("Muslims believe...") to second person ("You may have been taught...") might feel less clinical. This affects several callouts in Sessions 1, 3, 4, and 7.
Suggestion 8: Consider a note in Session 7 about the Qur'anic view of sin (lighter diagnosis)
Chapman observes that Islam's diagnosis of human nature is fundamentally more optimistic than Christianity's — sin is a failure of obedience rather than a corrupted nature, and human beings are not "fallen" in the Christian sense but simply forgetful and in need of guidance. This is worth one sentence of acknowledgement in the intro or bridge text of Session 7, because it explains why salvation-by-works feels so natural: if the problem is manageable, the solution should be too. A brief, non-condescending nod — "In the tradition many of us came from, the human being is not broken, just forgetful — capable of obedience if we try hard enough. That's a hopeful view of human nature. But the Bible sees the problem differently..." — bridges the gap without arguing.

Suggested improvements based on Miller
Session 1 — How Can We Know God? (Trusting the Bible)
Suggestion 1a — The Qur'an and the Bible are fundamentally different types of books
Miller makes a point that is practically very important and currently absent from the study: converts often read the Bible the way they read the Qur'an — as a single, timeless divine dictation rather than a story with historical context. A brief, non-technical note in the intro or a callout could flag this gently: "The Bible is a different kind of book from the Qur'an — not because it is less holy, but because God chose to speak through story, history, poetry, and letter, spread across centuries. Getting used to that is part of reading it well." This could address confusion early and frame the whole series better.
Suggestion 1b — Heart language
Miller notes that reading the Bible in a foreign language (rather than one's mother tongue) can subtly reinforce the idea that Christianity is foreign. The study could add a brief encouragement — perhaps in the Application Challenge — for participants to find the Bible in their heart language, not just in English.

Session 2 — Who is God? (The Personal God)
Suggestion 2a — The personality of God is different, not just his name
Miller's chapter 11 is one of the richest in the book. He argues that many converts assume the God of the Bible and the God of the Qur'an have the same personality — and that this assumption quietly distorts everything. His crisp formulation is worth adapting: "The Qur'an's god can forgive people, but you never know if he has forgiven you. The Bible's God loves all people, but more than that — he loves you, specifically, and you can know it." This distinction could be woven into the bridge text before the Psalm 103 questions, without any academic framing.
Suggestion 2c — The God who creates gradually and blesses
Miller points out that even Genesis 1 reveals something about God's personality that is surprising to Muslims: this God creates gradually, names things good, blesses the creatures, and rests. He shares authority with humanity. None of that matches the Qur'anic picture. The study doesn't have a Genesis session, but Session 2 is the natural home for a question like: "What does the fact that God called his creation 'good' — and blessed it — tell you about his character?" It's a simple addition but opens up something genuinely new.

Session 3 — Who is Jesus? (Part 1)
Suggestion 3a — The historical rootedness of the Christian confession
Miller notes that converts are often asked "How could you betray your people by leaving the true religion?" and have no good answer ready. Helping participants see that Christian faith has deep roots in the Middle East and North Africa — that it was there before Islam — is something the study doesn't address at all. This doesn't need to be a history lesson. A single sentence in the intro could do it: "Following Jesus is not adopting a foreign religion. The faith you have entered has been alive in the lands of the Middle East since the first century — long before Islam existed." This can be quietly powerful without becoming a history lecture.

Session 4 — The Trinity
Suggestion 4a — The Nicene Creed as a ready answer
This is Miller's most practically applicable insight for this session. He argues that converts are always, without exception, asked "What do you Christians believe? How can God be three?" — and that having a memorised answer is enormously helpful, especially in a charged or adversarial moment when composing a careful reply is very hard. The Nicene Creed, he notes, was specifically written to answer the question of the Son's relationship to the Father, and it does so in language that directly refutes the idea that "Son of God" implies a physical relationship. The study currently ends Session 4 with reflection questions. A simple addition could be: invite participants to read the Nicene Creed, and consider memorising it — framed not as a religious duty but as a gift: a ready, ancient answer for a moment when words might be hard to find.
Suggestion 4b — "Begotten, not made"
The Nicene Creed's phrase "begotten, not made, of one Being with the Father" directly addresses the most common Muslim objection ("Son of God means God had a son with Mary"). The study's existing misunderstanding callout on "How can God have a Son?" is good but brief. Miller's framing is cleaner for a popular audience: "'Begotten, not made' means Jesus did not come into existence the way created things do. He was not born. He was not manufactured. He has always existed in a relationship with the Father — that relationship is what 'Son' names." This could strengthen the existing callout without lengthening it much.

Session 7 — How Are We Saved? (Grace, Not Works)
Suggestion 7a — You can know you are forgiven
Miller's chapter 11 makes a contrast that belongs in Session 7 even more than Session 2: in Islam, you submit and obey, but you never know — and cannot know — whether God has accepted you. The Day of Judgement reveals it. Christian assurance is not arrogance; it is the direct opposite of the Islamic experience of radical uncertainty before God. The study touches on this in the leader notes but not in the main questions. A reflection question could make it personal: "In Islam, you can never be certain God has accepted you — only the Day of Judgement reveals that. What does it mean to you that God's acceptance is something you can know now, not something you have to wait to find out?"

Session 8 — What Does It Mean to Follow Jesus?
Suggestion 8a — Reading the Bible: observe, interpret, apply
Miller's chapter 3 describes a straightforward three-step approach to Bible reading (Observe, Interpret, Apply) and notes that without instruction, even a faithful churchgoer won't learn to actually use the Bible as a daily tool. Session 8 already has a section on practical rhythms including Bible reading, but it's somewhat general. A small addition to the Bible reading rhythm — something like "Good Bible reading has three moves: What does it say? What does it mean? What does it mean for me?" — would be immediately useful, memorable, and easy to include without disrupting the session's tone.
Suggestion 8b — Prayer: more than one form
Miller notes that evangelical prayer culture often means only one thing — extemporaneous prayer — and that this can be intimidating or alienating for someone coming from a structured prayer background. He suggests that teaching multiple forms of prayer (psalm-praying, set prayers, silent prayer, as well as spontaneous) is important. Session 8 currently frames prayer as "talking to God honestly." This is good. But a small addition acknowledging that there are different ways to pray — and that using written or structured prayers is not inferior, just different — could be freeing for participants who feel awkward with spontaneous prayer but have a deep instinct for structured devotion.

Session 9 — New Identity, New Family
Suggestion 9a — The community of the baptised across history
Miller describes the power of connecting CMBs to the historical and global church — the sense that you are not a tiny isolated group, but part of something stretching across centuries and continents. Session 9 is emotionally the heart of the series (the outline calls it the "emotional climax"), and it focuses well on belonging to God's family. But it could briefly touch on the historical dimension of that family: "You have not joined a new religion. You have joined a family that stretches back two thousand years, across every nation on earth — millions of people in every generation who have said the same things about Jesus, prayed to the same Father, and experienced the same Spirit." This connects beautifully to the Hebrews 12:1 "cloud of witnesses" image and adds a horizontal dimension (global, historical) to complement the vertical (God as Father) that the session already develops well.
Suggestion 9b — The Apostles' Creed as belonging
Miller notes that the Apostles' Creed was originally the baptismal confession — the words spoken when someone entered the community. Introducing it in Session 9 (rather than Session 4) could work powerfully: "Christians throughout history, in your land and in every land, were welcomed into God's family using these words. They are yours now too." This would give the creed a warmer, identity-forming function rather than a merely doctrinal one.

Session 10 — Living with Confidence
Suggestion 10a — Witness as life first, words second
Miller's chapter 15 makes the point that most conversions happen through affect (heart, relationship, the quality of a life) rather than argument — and that apologetics has a limited role: mainly to keep the conversation going rather than to win it. This is already present in the tone of Session 10, but Miller's framing is sharper and could be added to the misunderstanding callout on witness: "The purpose of having an answer is not to win an argument. It is to show that you have thought about these things — and to keep the door open for the relationship to continue. Changing someone's heart is God's work, not yours."
Suggestion 10b — Avoiding polemics when "coming out"
Miller's chapter 8 has a practical insight that doesn't appear in Session 10 at all: when a convert first reveals their faith to family, the worst thing they can do is begin by criticising Islam or Muhammad. The advice to lead with what you have found rather than what you have left is simple, tactically wise, and pastorally important. It could be added as a short practical note in the Application Challenge or in the leader notes: "When the moment comes to share your faith with your family, resist the urge to explain why Islam is wrong. Lead with what you have found — the love, the forgiveness, the certainty of belonging. Let them ask the harder questions in their own time."

Cross-cutting suggestions (any session could carry these)
Suggestion X1 — The liturgical calendar (Session 9 or Application Challenge)
Miller's chapter 9 makes a point that is easy to miss: Islamic holy days can be deeply painful for CMBs who can no longer participate in them but have no Christian equivalent to look forward to. Introducing the idea that Christianity has its own sacred rhythm — Advent, Christmas, Epiphany, Lent, Easter, Pentecost — could be a brief but meaningful addition to Session 9 or 10, framed as gift rather than obligation: "The Christian year gives your faith a rhythm — a pattern of seasons that shape the year around the story of Jesus."
Suggestion X2 — Identity, not just belief
Miller's central thesis — that the primary challenge for CMBs is not persecution but identity formation — is implicitly present throughout the study but never quite named. Making it explicit, perhaps in the Session 0 intro or the series introduction, could be surprisingly helpful: "This series is not only about what you now believe. It is about who you now are. Those are connected — but they're not the same thing. Over the coming sessions we'll be exploring both."

Suggestions based on Syrjänen
The Syrjänen study is different in kind from Madrigal. Madrigal provides theological content that can enrich specific sessions. Syrjänen provides pastoral and psychological insight about the people who might be sitting in front of you — what has driven them to faith, what they carry, what they fear, and what has actually moved them. His contribution is therefore less about what the study says and more about how it says it, and what it chooses to address.

Theme 1 — The road that led here: crisis, alienation, and the search for meaning
What Syrjänen found: Almost all the converts he interviewed had gone through a period of profound internal crisis before conversion. This was rarely a neat intellectual journey. It typically involved some combination of: deep personal distress (loss, failure, family breakdown), a sense that Islam was not answering their deepest questions, and a growing feeling of alienation — from their tradition, from their community, sometimes from God himself. Many had already tried everything their own tradition offered — shrines, prayers, mullahs — and found no peace. The recurring phrase in the testimonies was some form of: "There was no peace in my heart."
What this means for the study:
Suggestion 1 — Add a brief acknowledgement in Session 0 (or the intro to Session 5) that the road to faith is often through a dark place.
Currently: Session 0 is warm and welcoming but somewhat neutral about the emotional history that brings people there.
Suggested addition: A short bridge paragraph that names — without over-explaining — that many people come to faith through a period of real struggle: with questions that didn't get answered, with life circumstances that fell apart, with a search for something solid. Not as a therapy prompt, but as a moment of recognition. Something like: "Many people come to faith not in a calm moment, but in a difficult one — when life has raised questions that need answers, and old certainties have stopped being enough. If that's part of your story, you are not unusual. You are in good company."
This is pastoral more than theological. It tells participants they don't need to pretend their journey was straightforward.

Suggestion 2 — In Session 7 (Grace, Not Works), add a brief acknowledgement that the question of personal assurance is not just intellectual — it's often a deep ache.
Currently: Session 7 addresses assurance well theologically — it explains that assurance is grounded in God's promise, not our performance, and handles the "isn't certainty arrogant?" objection clearly.
What Syrjänen adds: His converts were not wrestling with assurance as an abstract theological puzzle. They were exhausted. They had tried to earn God's acceptance through discipline and religious practice and felt they were always falling short. The lack of assurance wasn't a doctrinal position — it was a lived experience of never knowing whether it was enough, whether they were enough. For some, physical disability made it worse: "If I am blind, I cannot offer even a chicken."
Suggested addition: A short bridge paragraph before the Likert scale, acknowledging that the longing for assurance is often not just intellectual but deeply personal — the exhaustion of never knowing if you've done enough, of carrying the weight of a standard you can't meet. This would give the theological content of Session 7 more emotional resonance. It's not a change in substance — it's a change in tone and point of contact.

Theme 2 — What the Bible has actually said to people
What Syrjänen found: The most consistently reported catalyst for conversion was encounter with the New Testament — particularly with Jesus' words in Matthew 11:28-30 ("Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest"). This verse appears again and again in the testimonies. Converts described reading it as a moment of role-identification: they saw themselves in the invitation. The paralysed man. The lost sheep. The one carrying a burden too heavy to bear. The text was not functioning primarily as doctrine — it was functioning as personal address.
What this means for the study:
Suggestion 3 — Elevate Matthew 11:28-30 in Session 8 (Discipleship).
Currently: Session 8 uses Matthew 11:28-30 but treats it as one of three passages, and the commentary focuses mainly on the concept of the "yoke" as a rabbi's teaching. It's good, but the passage is not given its full weight.
What Syrjänen's evidence suggests: For people from this background, this may be the single most powerful passage in the study. It addresses directly the exhaustion of religious effort, the weight of a system that always demands more, and the contrast with Jesus who gives rather than demands. It deserves more space — not as a theological point, but as a personal encounter. Consider letting participants sit with it longer, perhaps with a more extended question: "Read this again slowly. Is Jesus speaking to you? What burden are you carrying that he is inviting you to bring to him?"

Suggestion 4 — In Session 9 (New Identity, New Family), add Romans 8:15-16 as a primary passage rather than supporting text — and give the word "Abba" more weight.
Currently: The study uses the Romans 8 passage and briefly mentions Abba, but moves on fairly quickly.
What Syrjänen adds: The Mrs. Sheikh story he recounts is particularly illuminating. She couldn't call God "Father" at first — it felt presumptuous, even blasphemous. The idea that the transcendent God could be addressed with a word from family life struck her as impossible. Then one night, thinking of her own father's unconditional love for her as a child, something broke open and she found she could. The conversion of her prayer life was inseparable from the conversion of her understanding of God.
Suggested addition: A short piece of bridging text in Session 9 that names this honestly — that for many people from a Muslim background, calling God "Father" is not just theologically new but personally difficult. It can feel disrespectful, presumptuous, or even ridiculous at first. This is normal. It doesn't mean it's wrong. And for some people, the moment it becomes possible — the moment they can actually address God that way and mean it — is the moment something deep shifts. This acknowledges a real obstacle rather than glossing over it, and makes the session more pastorally useful.

Theme 3 — Dreams, visions, and experiences that don't fit neatly
What Syrjänen found: About half of his 36 interviewees reported some form of supernatural experience — dream, vision, or audition — as part of their conversion journey. These were not marginal cases or credulous people. They included educated professionals, a former mullah, and a former pir. The experiences varied widely but shared a common pattern: they came at a moment of tension and decision, they brought a sense of peace or resolution, and they typically pointed the person toward Christ. Several converts said explicitly that their dream or vision had given them the courage or "licence" to act on what they already believed but were afraid to do.
What this means for the study:
Suggestion 5 — Add a short callout; subtype: general in Session 0 or early in Session 1 that gently normalises the possibility of non-cognitive pathways to faith.
Currently: The study consistently frames coming to faith as an intellectual and spiritual journey involving questions, Scripture, and personal reflection. This is exactly right for a study format — but it may inadvertently suggest that the only "real" or "respectable" path to faith is an intellectual one.
Suggested addition: A low-key acknowledgement — perhaps in the Session 0 welcome, or in the opening of Session 1 — that God meets people in different ways. Some through argument and evidence. Some through a dream or an experience they can't fully explain. Some through the life of another person. Some through a crisis. None of these is more or less valid than the others. What matters is where you've arrived, not the route.
This is a pastoral note more than a theological claim, and it would be valued by participants whose journey included an experience they may feel embarrassed to mention in a study-group setting.

Theme 4 — The cost of belonging to the new community
What Syrjänen found: The period after conversion — what he calls the "period of incorporation" — was often the hardest. Converts faced rejection from family, social ostracism, loss of employment, and in some cases physical danger. More subtly, they also faced the challenge of integrating into a Pakistani Christian community that carried its own social stigma (associated with the historically low-caste background of most Pakistani Christians). Some felt they had left one set of difficulties only to find another. The community they were joining did not always feel like home.
What this means for the study:
Suggestion 6 — Session 9 (New Identity, New Family) already handles this well, but could be strengthened by one addition: naming the possibility that the new family itself is imperfect and will sometimes disappoint.
Currently: Session 9 does acknowledge loss and grief honestly, and it doesn't pretend belonging is automatic. The existing text in the closing section is pastoral and good.
Suggested addition: A short, honest sentence in the "Big Picture Truths" section, something like: "The church is a community of people still being shaped by grace — it will sometimes disappoint you, and you may sometimes disappoint it. That is not a reason to withdraw. It is an invitation to stay and to extend the same grace that has been shown to you."
This is not new theologically — but naming it directly would reduce the risk of disillusionment when the reality of imperfect community sets in.

Theme 5 — The fear that conversion means becoming a chuhra
What Syrjänen found: One of the most significant practical barriers to conversion in Pakistani culture was the strong identification of Christianity with the lowest-caste Chuhra background. To become a Christian was, in the minds of many, to become socially degraded — to join the sweepers. This identification was so powerful that several converts reported being told they had "become a chuhra" rather than "become a Christian." The social shame attached to this was, for many, more immediately threatening than theological objections.
What this means for the study: This is Pakistan-specific and may not translate directly to every context. However, the principle may apply more broadly: people in your group may carry fears that conversion means losing social standing, dignity, or the respect of people they care about — not just family relations, but community reputation.
Suggestion 7 — In Session 8 (Discipleship) or Session 9, add a brief acknowledgement that following Jesus may involve accepting a lower social position in the eyes of some.
Currently: Session 8 addresses the cost of discipleship honestly via Mark 8:34-38 — it does not minimise the real losses. This is already well done.
Suggested addition: A single question or bridging sentence that names, with specificity, that the cost for some people is not just family conflict but social standing — the way you are perceived, the respect you used to have, the identity you were proud of. Jesus was not unaware of this. He was himself rejected, associated with the wrong people, and died a criminal's death. He does not ask you to bear shame he was not willing to bear himself.
This is a pastoral deepening of what's already there, rather than a structural change.


Additional Suggested Changes from the Syrjänen Book for Session 5 specifically
1. Enrich the opening illustration with the specific texture of felt experience (Medium-high impact)
The current doctor illustration is effective but generic. Syrjänen's interviews reveal something very specific about how Pakistani Muslim converts actually experienced the problem the session is addressing: not as a theological concept but as a felt, embodied restlessness. Converts repeatedly described it in three recurring Urdu phrases — no peace of mind (sakūn), no satisfaction (tasallī), no rest (itminān) — even after sincere religious practice.
This points to a gap in the current introduction. The study tells participants what the Bible teaches about sin being internal, but it doesn't first validate what they may have already felt. A short addition to the intro — something like: "Many people who have taken their faith seriously describe a restlessness that sincere religious effort couldn't reach. They prayed, they fasted, they tried harder — and still something felt unresolved. That experience is not a failure of devotion. The Bible's diagnosis of sin explains it: the problem runs deeper than effort can reach" — would do two things at once. It honours the reader's own religious seriousness, and it connects the theological diagnosis to something they may already recognise from inside.

2. Add a new Common Question addressing the sufficiency of religious practice (High impact)
Syrjänen's material reveals a pattern across multiple converts: people who had performed all the required duties — prayer five times daily, fasting, pilgrimage — and still found no peace. One interviewee put it precisely: "I had led a good life and even so I saw how many times I had sinned by thought, deed and action... I felt that if I am disobedient to law, then I am a sinner before God." Another: "I said the prayers, I observed the fast fully, I worshipped — this all I did... I did not have any satisfaction."
This is the exact pastoral territory the session is covering, but there is currently no Common Question that speaks to it from this angle. The existing Common Question about sincerity ("But surely God rewards those who try hard?") addresses the intellectual objection but not the experiential one. A new question could sit alongside it:

"I have prayed sincerely, fasted, and tried to live correctly. Why isn't that enough?"
The Bible's answer here is not that those things don't matter — they reflect real devotion and a genuine desire to know God. But Romans 3 is pointing to something deeper: that the problem is not a shortfall in effort, but a condition of the heart that effort alone cannot fix. Think of it this way: washing the outside of a cup doesn't clean what is on the inside. No amount of external obedience can reach the internal disorder that Jesus describes in Mark 7. This is not meant to discourage. It is meant to explain why, even after sincere effort, something still feels unresolved — and to point toward the only solution that actually reaches deep enough.


3. Add a sentence to the Isaiah 59 section connecting separation to the felt experience of unanswered prayer (Low-medium impact)
Syrjänen's converts frequently described their religious crisis in terms of God not responding — going to shrines, consulting religious scholars, praying intensely, and feeling that nothing connected. One convert said plainly: "As long as religion cannot give us peace, it will not pass the test." Another: "God gives everybody food and drink, why does he not give to me? If I have done something wrong, I now repent."
Isaiah 59:2 — "your sinful acts have alienated you from your God; your sins have caused him to hide his face from you so that he does not hear" — speaks directly to this experience. The current bridge text before that passage is good, but a single sentence could make the connection explicit: "Many people describe a religious life in which prayer felt like it was going nowhere — as though God was not listening. Isaiah names that experience directly, and gives it a theological explanation." This validates a specific felt experience before presenting the biblical text, which is more effective pastorally than presenting the text cold.

4. Strengthen the framework section with the works/grace contrast drawn from actual convert experience (Medium impact)
The current framework section presents "a common instinct" vs "what the Bible teaches" in a clean summary form. Syrjänen's material reveals how viscerally people felt the weight of the works-based system — the endless conditionality of it: eat only halal food, and then your prayers are valid; and then your worship is accepted; and then perhaps salvation follows — with the haunting uncertainty that it might still not be enough. One convert summarised it: "Salvation is very difficult." Another, who was blind, put it movingly: "When I am helpless, I cannot offer even a chicken — if I remain (without grace), perhaps I am not saved."
The current framework could be sharpened by adding a brief, concrete description of what it actually feels like to live inside a works-based framework — not as a caricature, but as a sympathetic recognition of the burden. Something like: "A life of careful obedience — where each action must be correct, where salvation depends on the accumulation of righteous deeds, where the outcome is never certain until the end — is an exhausting place to live. The Bible does not dismiss that effort. But it offers something different: not a higher standard to reach, but a gift to receive."
