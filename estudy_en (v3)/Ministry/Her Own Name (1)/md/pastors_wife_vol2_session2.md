[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el01

Mama Bello had never stood behind a pulpit. She had never been invited to speak at a conference or lead a women's retreat. She did not have a title. She had been attending the same church for thirty-one years, and for most of those years she had done one thing with quiet, relentless consistency: she had found the younger women, and she had sat with them.

She sat with them when their husbands were difficult and when their children were sick and when they did not know how to pray. She opened the Bible with them at her kitchen table, unhurried, asking questions more often than she gave answers. She had no programme and no curriculum. She simply gave time — and honesty — and the kind of attention that made people feel they were the only person in the room.

Adaeze — whose story we met in Session 2 of Volume 1 — had been one of Mama Bello's women. Fifteen years later, Adaeze was doing the same thing with three younger women in her own church, sitting at her own kitchen table, asking the same kind of questions. She had not planned it. She had simply been formed by it.

This is discipleship. Not a programme. A life, passed on.

This session is about what it means to invest in other women — to teach, to disciple, to pass on what has been given to you. It is a calling that every pastor's wife has access to, whatever her gifts, whatever her formal role, whatever the size of her husband's church. And it is one of the most significant things she will ever do.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — The Titus 2 Pattern

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el03

Paul's letter to Titus contains one of the most direct passages in the New Testament about women teaching women. It is brief — just three verses — but it carries a weight of implication that is easy to miss on a quick reading. It describes not a programme but a pattern: older women, shaped by their own experience of grace, investing that experience in younger women. The church's health, in Paul's vision, depends on this pattern functioning well.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — What Is Passed On

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el09

Paul's second letter to Timothy opens with a remarkable passage about intergenerational faith. Timothy is a young church leader, and Paul is writing to encourage him. But before he says anything about Timothy's ministry or his doctrine, he reaches back two generations — to Timothy's grandmother Lois and his mother Eunice — and names them as the source of the faith now burning in Timothy.

The faith that changed the world in Timothy's generation was planted in a household by two women whose names appear nowhere else in Scripture.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el15
**EYEBROW:** Worth Examining
**TERM:** "I don't know enough to teach anyone."
**QUESTION:** Do you need formal theological training before you can disciple someone?
**ANSWER:** No — and the expectation that you do is one of the things that keeps the Titus 2 pattern from functioning as it should in many churches.

Discipleship, at its most basic, is not the transfer of theological information. It is the sharing of a life — a life that has been shaped by Scripture, by prayer, by failure and recovery, by the slow work of the Holy Spirit over time. The woman who sits with a younger woman and opens the Bible with her, asks honest questions, shares her own struggles and what she has learned from them, and prays — that woman is doing something that no formal qualification can replace.

This does not mean content does not matter. It does. Growing in your own knowledge of Scripture, your own understanding of the gospel, your own ability to read a passage carefully — all of this makes you a better disciple of others. The investment in your own formation that Paul describes in Titus 2:3 is not optional.

But "I don't know enough" is almost always a statement about confidence rather than competence. If you have followed Jesus for more than a few years, you know more than a new believer. If you have navigated a difficult marriage, you have something to offer a younger wife. If you have come through grief or illness or a season of doubt and found God faithful, that testimony is worth more in the life of someone facing the same thing than any textbook.

You know enough. Begin.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — The First Witnesses

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el17

In Session 1 of Volume 1, we noted that the first person Jesus appeared to after the resurrection was a woman — Mary Magdalene — and that he sent her to carry the resurrection news to the disciples. That commissioning deserves a closer look in the context of teaching and witness.

Mary is sent. She goes. She announces. The disciples do not initially believe her (Luke 24:11) — but she says it anyway. She is the first teacher of the central truth of the Christian faith: *He is risen.*

This pattern — women as primary witnesses and transmitters of gospel truth — is not incidental in Luke's Gospel. Luke is the most careful historian among the Gospel writers, and he records the women's role with deliberate precision.

**TEXT:** Part 4 — Teaching in the Grain of Daily Life

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el22

The most famous passage in all of Jewish Scripture about the transmission of faith is not about a school or a synagogue. It is about a family, going about ordinary life — walking, sitting, lying down, getting up. The Shema — Israel's central confession of faith — was to be spoken into the rhythm of daily existence, woven into the texture of ordinary time.

The model is not a programme. It is a practice of presence.

[type: callout; subtype: general]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el27
**EYEBROW:** A Practical Note
**TERM:** What does discipleship actually look like week to week?
**QUESTION:** Discipleship sounds important — but what does it actually look like in practice, for a pastor's wife with limited time and energy?
**ANSWER:** It does not have to be complicated. In fact, the simpler the better.

Here are some practical shapes it can take:

**One-to-one over a regular meeting** — coffee, a meal, a walk. A consistent time, roughly fortnightly or monthly, with the same person. Open the Bible together, ask how she is doing, pray before you part. That is enough to start.

**Reading the same thing** — going through a book of the Bible or a short Christian book together, each reading a chapter between meetings, then discussing what struck you. The material gives the conversation structure without requiring you to be an expert.

**Being available at a specific time** — not always-on availability, which is unsustainable, but a named, regular slot when people know they can reach you. This protects your margin while creating genuine access.

**Inviting in rather than scheduling out** — including a younger woman in something you are already doing: accompanying you on a visit, sitting with you during a church event, watching how you handle a pastoral situation. Formation through proximity, not only through formal teaching.

**Being honest about your own life** — sharing what you are learning, what you are struggling with, how you are finding God faithful. Discipleship is not mentorship from a position of arrived perfection. It is one traveller sharing the road with another.

The most important thing is consistency over time. A shallow but consistent investment will almost always outperform an intense but occasional one.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch08-el33

You have received something. A faith that was planted in you, nurtured in you, kept alive in you — by Scripture, by prayer, by the Spirit of God, and almost certainly by the faithfulness of other people. Some of them are named in your memory. Some, like Lois and Eunice, are the unnamed ones whose consistency made everything else possible.

You are not at the end of that chain. You are in the middle of it. What was given to you is not yours to keep. It is yours to pass on — in whatever shape, at whatever pace, through whatever relationship God places in your hands.

The kitchen table is enough. The honest conversation is enough. The open Bible and the unhurried question are enough. Mama Bello knew that. Lois knew it. Eunice knew it.

Now it is your turn.

**EYEBROW:** Application Challenge
**TERM:** Before Session 3
**QUESTION:** What is your challenge for this week?
**ANSWER:** Three things — choose at least one.

**If you are not yet discipling anyone:** identify one woman this week — someone you already know, someone a few steps behind you in some area of life or faith — and take one concrete step toward her. Send a message. Suggest a coffee. Make it simple and low-pressure. You are not proposing a formal programme. You are offering friendship with intention.

**If you are already investing in someone:** this week, before you meet with her, spend ten minutes asking God what she most needs from you right now — not what you planned to say, but what she needs. Then ask her, at the start of your time together: *"How are you — really?"* And give her enough time to answer.

**For everyone:** write down the name of the person who most shaped your faith — your Mama Bello, your Lois, your Eunice. If they are still alive, send them a message this week telling them what they gave you. If they are not, write it down anyway — and let it remind you of the weight of what you carry, and what you are responsible for passing on.
