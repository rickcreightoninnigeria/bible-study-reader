[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el01

At the end of Session 1, you were asked to write one sentence: *"In Christ, I am…"*

If you did it, you may have found it harder than expected. Many women find it surprisingly difficult to complete that sentence without slipping back into what they *do* — "I am a helper," "I am a servant," "I am a minister's wife" — rather than who they *are*.

That difficulty is worth paying attention to.

Adaeze had been a believer for over fifteen years. She could preach on the love of God with conviction. She had led women's retreats on God's grace. She could quote John 3:16 and Romans 8:38–39 from memory. But one evening, after a particularly wounding remark from an older woman in the congregation — *"You don't really have the gift of hospitality, do you?"* — she found herself weeping in her bedroom, wondering whether God was as disappointed in her as that woman seemed to be.

The truth she knew in her head had not yet reached the deepest part of her heart.

That is what this session is about. Not just knowing *about* God's love, but being known *by* it — personally, specifically, all the way down.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — Searched and Known

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el03

The application challenge from Session 1 pointed you to Psalm 139. We begin there now — because before we can receive love well, we need to grasp what it means to be truly *known*.

Many of us have a complicated relationship with being known. Being known means being seen — and being seen can feel dangerous when we are not sure we will be accepted for what is found. We manage our image. We present our best side. In ministry contexts especially, we learn to project competence and composure.

The psalmist has no such option. And he discovers that is not a threat — it is a relief.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — The Woman at the Well

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el10

Psalm 139 gives us the theological foundation: God knows us completely. The Gospels show us what that looks like when it walks into a specific person's life.

The account of Jesus and the woman at the well is one of the longest and most detailed one-on-one conversations Jesus has with anyone in the Gospels. His conversation partner is a Samaritan woman — someone who would have been excluded from the religious life of her day on three counts: her ethnicity, her gender, and her personal history.

Jesus chose her. He sought her out. He spoke to her first.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el15
**EYEBROW:** Worth Reflecting On
**TERM:** "I have to keep up appearances for the congregation."
**QUESTION:** Is there pressure in your context to present a version of yourself that is more put-together than the reality? What does that cost you?
**ANSWER:** There is a real and legitimate sense in which a pastor and his family set a tone for a congregation. Character matters. Conduct matters. But there is a significant difference between *integrity* — living consistently and honestly — and *performance* — projecting an image that does not match reality.

When a pastor's wife feels she must hide her marriage difficulties, her doubts, her grief, or her struggles with sin in order to protect the ministry's reputation, something has gone wrong. The congregation is not being led — it is being deceived. And the pastor's wife is being slowly hollowed out.

The gospel does not require image management. It requires honesty before God and, in appropriate ways, before one another. Paul wrote to the Corinthians out of "great distress and anguish of heart and with many tears" (2 Corinthians 2:4). He did not hide his struggles from the churches he served. He brought them into the light.

The woman at the well was known — completely — and became a witness. Not in spite of being known, but because of it.

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el17

There is another woman in Luke's Gospel who encounters Jesus — and her story approaches the question of love from a completely different angle. She does not have a long conversation with him. She does not become a witness or a teacher. She simply weeps at his feet, and he defends her.

That, too, is a picture of what it means to be loved by God.

**TEXT:** Part 4 — Nothing Can Separate You

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el23

The three passages we have read so far — Psalm 139, John 4, Luke 7 — all approach the same reality from different angles: God's knowledge of you is complete, his pursuit of you is deliberate, and his love for you is not conditional on your performance or your reputation.

Paul, in Romans 8, brings this to its fullest and most sweeping expression. After eleven chapters of careful theological argument about the human condition and God's rescue, he pauses to ask a series of rhetorical questions — and to answer them with something approaching amazed disbelief that the answer is as good as it is.

[type: callout; subtype: general]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el28
**EYEBROW:** A Note on Receiving
**TERM:** Learning to receive love, not just give it
**QUESTION:** Why do people in ministry sometimes find it hard to receive care, rather than always being the one who gives it?
**ANSWER:** People in ministry — pastors, their wives, church workers — often develop a strong identity around *giving*. They visit the sick, counsel the struggling, pray for the grieving. Over time, this can make it genuinely difficult to be on the receiving end. Receiving help can feel like weakness. Receiving care can feel uncomfortable, even inappropriate for someone in a leadership role.

But the ability to receive love — from God and from other people — is not a weakness. It is a spiritual discipline. Mary of Bethany sat and received from Jesus while Martha served. Jesus received the ministrations of the woman with the alabaster jar. Paul received financial support from the Philippian church with gratitude rather than embarrassment (Philippians 4:15–16).

If you find it hard to let people care for you, it is worth asking why. Sometimes it is pride. Sometimes it is a fear of being seen as needy. Sometimes it is simply a role-identity that has become too rigid. Whatever the reason, learning to receive well is part of being known and loved — and part of what makes genuine community possible.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch02-el34

You are not loved because you are useful. You are not known because you are impressive. You are not accepted because you have held it together.

God searched you and knew you before you were formed. Jesus sought you out and named you — like Mary in the garden, like the woman at the well, like the woman at Simon's table. And nothing — not failure, not criticism, not a difficult season of ministry, not a wound from within the church — nothing can undo what God has declared over you in Christ.

The role will make demands on you. The congregation will have opinions. But the one who made you, knows you, and loves you has already said everything that matters.

You are his. Fully. Finally. Forever.

**EYEBROW:** Application Challenge
**TERM:** Before Session 3
**QUESTION:** What is your challenge for this week?
**ANSWER:** Choose one of the four passages from this session and read it every day this week — not to prepare anything, not to teach it, but simply to let it speak to you.

As you read, try to receive it personally. Put your name into the text. Psalm 139:1 becomes: *"O Lord, you have searched Adaeze and known her."* Romans 8:38 becomes: *"I am convinced that nothing can separate Adaeze from the love of God."*

This is not a technique — it is a practice of letting the truth move from your head to your heart. Many women find that reading the same passage repeatedly across a week does something that reading many passages once cannot.
