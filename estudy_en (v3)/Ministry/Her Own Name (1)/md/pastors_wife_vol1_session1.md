[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el01

Ngozi had been a pastor's wife for eleven years. She could tell you the order of service by heart. She knew which women needed a visit, which families were struggling, which deacons needed managing. She ran the women's fellowship, organised the children's Christmas programme, led the choir practice when the choir director was absent, and made sure there was always food after the evening service.

She was, by every measure, excellent at the job.

But one Tuesday morning, sitting in the empty church after everyone had left, she realised something had gone quietly wrong. She could not remember the last time she had simply sat with Jesus — not as a pastor's wife preparing for a meeting, not as a leader looking for a verse to share, but as herself. As Ngozi.

*Who am I*, she wondered, *when I am not being useful?*

That is the question this session begins with. Not "What does a pastor's wife do?" but something more foundational: *Who are you — to God?*

[type: heading; subtype: section]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el02
**TEXT:** Part 1 — The Danger of Performing

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el03

There is a particular pressure that comes with being married to a pastor. The congregation has expectations. The wider community has expectations. Sometimes the denomination has expectations. And often, underneath all of those, a woman has quietly built up a set of expectations for herself — a standard she feels she must meet in order to be worthy of the role.

This is not unique to Nigeria, but it takes on particular shape in traditional contexts. A pastor's wife may feel she must dress a certain way, speak a certain way, raise perfect children, never show weakness, and always be available. If she falls short of any of these — even in small ways — she may feel not just inadequate, but *unfaithful*.

The Bible has a word for building your identity on your performance. Jesus used it of the Pharisees.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el07
**EYEBROW:** Cultural Pressure
**TERM:** "A good pastor's wife must always be strong."
**QUESTION:** Have you ever heard — or believed — that showing weakness as a pastor's wife is a kind of failure? Where do you think that idea comes from?
**ANSWER:** The Bible never calls any believer to project strength they do not have. In fact, the apostle Paul says the opposite: "When I am weak, then I am strong" (2 Corinthians 12:10). The expectation of unbroken strength is not a biblical standard — it is often a cultural one, sometimes reinforced by church tradition, sometimes by what we see modelled around us.

A pastor's wife who hides her struggles does not protect the congregation. She teaches them, by example, that the Christian life is about appearances. And she cuts herself off from the grace, prayer, and support she genuinely needs.

Strength that comes from Christ is a real and wonderful thing. But it is not the same as never admitting difficulty. Jesus wept (John 11:35). Paul listed his sufferings honestly (2 Corinthians 11:24–28). The Bible is consistently honest about human weakness — because the gospel is for weak people.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — What Jesus Thought of Women

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el09

Before we can talk about who you are as a pastor's wife, we need to talk about who you are to Jesus. And to understand that, it helps to notice how Jesus actually treated women — because it was not what his culture expected.

In first-century Jewish and Roman society, women had limited public standing. They could not testify in court. They were not formally educated in the Torah. Their place was in the household, not in public religious life.

Jesus stepped straight across all of that.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — Your Identity in Christ

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el18

The New Testament is bold about what happens to a person when they come to faith in Jesus. Your status before God is not based on your role, your family, your performance, or the expectations of your congregation. It is based entirely on what God has done for you in Christ.

This is not a minor theological point. It is the foundation on which everything else in your life — including your ministry — must be built. When that foundation is wrong, everything built on top of it becomes unstable.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el24
**EYEBROW:** Worth Thinking About
**TERM:** "My identity is tied to my husband's ministry."
**QUESTION:** Is it wrong for a pastor's wife to find meaning in her husband's ministry?
**ANSWER:** It is not wrong to find joy and meaning in supporting your husband's calling. Marriage is a partnership, and sharing in ministry can be a genuine source of purpose and delight.

The danger is when that becomes the *foundation* of your identity — so that when the ministry goes through a hard season, or your husband faces criticism, or a church plant fails to grow, you feel as though *you* have failed, or as though *your worth* is in question.

Your identity in Christ is not borrowed from your husband. You are not "chosen in him" because you married a pastor. You are chosen in Christ because God set his love on *you* — personally, before the world was made (Ephesians 1:4). That identity cannot be taken from you by a difficult deacons' meeting, an ungrateful congregation, or a season of ministry disappointment.

The ministry may change. Your husband's role may change. Your identity in Christ does not.

[type: heading; subtype: section]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el25
**TEXT:** Part 4 — The Role That Was Never Specified

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el26

Here is a fact that may surprise you: the Bible never describes the role of a pastor's wife.

The New Testament has a great deal to say about pastors and elders — their character, their family life, their teaching responsibilities. It has a great deal to say about women in the church — their dignity, their gifts, their ministry. But it nowhere defines "pastor's wife" as a church office, a specific role, or a set of required duties.

That means that every list of expectations placed on you — written or unwritten, spoken or assumed — comes from somewhere other than Scripture. It may come from culture, from tradition, from previous models of ministry, from your mother-in-law, or from your own imagination of what the role should look like. But it does not come directly from God.

This is not a licence to disengage from church life. It is an invitation to engage on a different basis — from identity, not from duty; from love, not from fear; from freedom, not from performance.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el29
**EYEBROW:** Family Pressure
**TERM:** "But our family has always done it this way."
**QUESTION:** What do you do when the expectations placed on you as a pastor's wife come not from the church but from your own family — or your husband's family?
**ANSWER:** In many Nigerian communities, the extended family has a strong voice in how a household is run — and a pastor's household is no exception. In-laws may have firm ideas about what a good wife does, what she wears, how she spends her time, whether she works outside the home, how quickly she has children, and how involved she is in her husband's ministry.

When those expectations are wise and loving, they are a gift. But when they conflict with your calling, your gifts, or your wellbeing, they deserve to be weighed carefully — not simply accepted because they are tradition.

The Bible calls a man to "leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife" (Genesis 2:24). That verse is about much more than geography. It describes a primary loyalty. A husband and wife together seek God's will for their household — and that is not a decision made by committee.

This does not mean cutting off family relationships. It means that God's call on your life, and your husband's life, takes first place. Family expectations, however sincerely held, are secondary to that.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch01-el35

You did not become valuable when you became a pastor's wife. You were already known, already chosen, already loved — before the congregation existed, before your husband was called, before you said yes to any of it.

The role is real. The responsibilities matter. The ministry is a high and beautiful calling. But none of that is the foundation of who you are. The foundation is this: you are a daughter of God, chosen in Christ before the foundation of the world, fully accepted by the one whose opinion matters most.

Everything else — the serving, the leading, the giving, the enduring — flows from that. Or it should. That is what the rest of these sessions are about.
