[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch04-el01

Chisom had a reputation in the diocese. People said she was everything a pastor's wife should be: well-dressed, well-spoken, gracious under pressure, always present, always available, always composed. Other pastors' wives were sometimes pointed to her as an example.

What they did not see was Sunday evening. The weariness that came over her the moment the last person left. The irritability she directed at her children that she would never have shown to a church member. The way she scrolled her phone for an hour rather than pray, because she had nothing left. The resentment she had stopped examining because examining it felt too dangerous.

She was not a hypocrite in the calculated sense — she was not deliberately deceiving anyone. She had simply learned, over years, to maintain the surface so well that she had lost track of what was underneath it.

The hidden life — the life of the heart, the interior life that only God sees — is what this session is about. Not because the outward life doesn't matter, but because the outward life eventually becomes whatever the hidden life actually is. You cannot maintain a gap between them indefinitely. And in ministry, the gap, when it collapses, tends to collapse in public.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — The Danger of the Cleaned-Up Outside

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch04-el09

If 1 Samuel 16 shows us what God looks for, Matthew 23 shows us what he finds most troubling — and it is not immorality or theological error, but something subtler: the careful maintenance of an outward appearance that no longer matches the inward reality.

Jesus reserved his sharpest words for religious leaders who had mastered the exterior while neglecting the interior. His critique is not aimed at pagans or obvious sinners. It is aimed at people deeply embedded in religious life — people who prayed, fasted, gave, and served, but whose inner life had become, in his words, full of "hypocrisy and lawlessness."

This is a passage for people in ministry. It is uncomfortable precisely because it is so close to home.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch04-el13
**EYEBROW:** Worth Sitting With
**TERM:** "I have to hold it together for everyone else."
**QUESTION:** Is it part of a pastor's wife's calling to always appear strong and composed — for the sake of the congregation?
**ANSWER:** There is a version of this that is genuinely virtuous: choosing not to unload personal struggles on people who are already carrying heavy burdens; maintaining composure in moments of pastoral crisis when others need steadiness; protecting the congregation from unnecessary worry. Discretion and self-discipline are real gifts.

But there is another version that is quietly destructive: never allowing anyone to see beneath the surface; treating vulnerability as a threat to be managed rather than a need to be met; maintaining the appearance of strength long past the point where it is honest.

The Bible draws a consistent line between appropriate discretion and the kind of performance that becomes its own form of pride. Maintaining a composed exterior while the interior is in distress is not strength — it is a form of isolation, and it tends to produce exactly the brittleness it is designed to prevent.

Jesus did not hold it together for everyone else. He wept at Lazarus's tomb. He was "troubled in spirit" before his arrest (John 13:21). He cried out from the cross. His composure, when he showed it, was real — rooted in genuine trust in the Father, not in an unwillingness to be seen.

The congregation does not need a pastor's wife who is always composed. It needs one who is genuinely rooted — and there is a difference.

**TEXT:** Part 4 — The Disciplines of the Hidden Life

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch04-el21

In the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus addresses three classic practices of Jewish religious life — giving, prayer, and fasting — and in each case he makes the same point: when these things are done for an audience, they lose their power. They become performance rather than encounter. The discipline that was meant to shape the interior gets redirected toward the exterior, and the soul is left hungry.

His alternative is not to stop giving, praying, or fasting. It is to do them *in secret* — in a place where only God sees, where there is no audience to impress and no reputation to protect.

[type: callout; subtype: general]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch04-el26
**EYEBROW:** A Honest Question
**TERM:** What is actually in the cup?
**QUESTION:** Jesus says to clean the inside of the cup. Before we can clean it, we need to know what is in it. What is a useful way to examine the state of your own interior life?
**ANSWER:** Honest self-examination is not the same as self-criticism. It is not about cataloguing failures or talking yourself into shame. It is simply about asking: *What is actually true about my inner life right now?*

Some useful questions to sit with:
- What do I find myself thinking about most, when no one is watching?
- What emotions am I regularly suppressing or avoiding?
- When I serve, what do I most want in return — and what does that tell me?
- Is there someone I have not forgiven, or a resentment I have stopped examining?
- What do I do when I am tired and no one is looking — and what does that reveal?
- When I am alone with God, is that a relief or an awkwardness?

These questions are not meant to produce anxiety. They are meant to produce honesty — the kind of honesty that makes it possible to bring the real you to God, rather than the managed version.

The tax collector did not examine himself extensively. He simply stopped hiding. That is the starting point.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch04-el32

The congregation sees your Sunday. God sees your Tuesday. Your Sunday can be good and real and genuinely honouring to God — but it cannot stay that way indefinitely if Tuesday is empty.

The hidden life is not a bonus for especially spiritual people. It is the root system. Everything that is visible above the ground depends on what is happening underneath it, in the dark, unseen. A tree with no root system can look healthy for a season — but it cannot stand when the wind comes.

You are in ministry for the long haul. The wind will come. What you tend now, in the hidden places — in the quiet, in the honest prayer, in the private disciplines that no one sees — that is what will hold you.

God sees in secret. And what he sees, he tends.

**EYEBROW:** Application Challenge
**TERM:** Before Session 5
**QUESTION:** What is your challenge for this week?
**ANSWER:** Set aside fifteen minutes this week for what you might call a *quiet audit* — not a time of intercession or preparation, but a time of honest sitting before God.

Use the questions from the callout in Part 4 as a starting point. Ask God to show you what is actually in the cup — not to condemn you, but to begin the work of cleaning from the inside.

Then take one thing you find — one emotion, one pattern, one unexamined corner — and simply name it before God. You do not need to fix it in one session. You just need to stop pretending it is not there.

The tax collector's prayer is always available: *God, be merciful to me, a sinner.* It is the prayer that goes home justified.

Bring one honest reflection from your week — not a polished one — to the start of Session 5.
