[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el01

The message came through a third party, as these things often do.

Sister Comfort — a long-standing member of the women's fellowship, someone Taiwo had considered a friend — had apparently been telling other women that Taiwo was proud, that she thought herself better than everyone else since her husband became senior pastor, and that she had changed. The women who heard it were divided: some dismissed it, some repeated it, and at least one came directly to Taiwo to tell her what was being said. That woman meant well. The telling made everything worse.

Taiwo spent three days in a state she could only describe as a slow fire. She was not sure which was stronger: the hurt of being misrepresented, the anger at the disloyalty, or the cold suspicion — which she hated herself for feeling — that somewhere underneath Sister Comfort's words there might be a grain of truth she would have to examine.

She did not know what to do. Confronting Sister Comfort directly felt dangerous. Ignoring it felt like surrender. Talking to her husband about it would drag him into something he did not need. Talking to anyone else in the church felt like adding to the very problem she was trying to stop.

She prayed. The fire did not go out immediately. But she stayed at the prayer long enough to ask a question she had not asked before: *Lord, what do you want to do with this — not just with Sister Comfort, but with me?*

That question, it turned out, was the beginning of the way through.

This session is about conflict: the gossip that travels through a congregation before you know it has started, the manipulation that is hard to name because it is always deniable, the misunderstanding that calcifies into resentment, the family pressure that arrives from outside the church and lands inside your marriage. These are not exotic problems. They are the ordinary furniture of ministry life. And the Bible has more to say about navigating them than most churches teach.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — When Someone Has Wronged You

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el03

Matthew 18 contains the most direct teaching Jesus gives about what to do when a fellow believer has wronged you. It is a passage that is frequently quoted and less frequently followed — partly because it asks for something that feels unnatural and costly, and partly because it runs against the instinct, common in many cultures, to manage conflict indirectly rather than address it face to face.

Jesus' model is disarmingly simple. And it is worth noticing who he is addressing: not a community leader managing church disputes, but any person who has been wronged. The responsibility to act belongs to the one who has been hurt.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — Gossip and the Slow Fire

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el09

Gossip is not a minor vice. In the New Testament it appears in lists alongside sexual immorality, idolatry, and murder — not because it is equally harmful in every instance, but because it is a symptom of the same disordered heart, and because its effects on community are deeply destructive. Proverbs, with its characteristic concrete imagery, describes what gossip does with particular vividness.

For a pastor's wife, gossip presents a specific double problem: she is more likely than most to be the subject of it, and she has access — through her husband's confidences and her own pastoral relationships — to information that could fuel it. Both vulnerabilities require attention.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el14
**EYEBROW:** Worth Naming
**TERM:** "It's not gossip if it's true."
**QUESTION:** Is sharing true but private information about someone acceptable — especially in a church context?
**ANSWER:** This is one of the most common rationalisations for gossip — and one of the most damaging.

Truth is not the only criterion for whether information should be shared. The other questions are: Does the person sharing it have a right to share it? Does the person receiving it need to know it? Will sharing it serve the other person's good — or will it primarily serve the speaker's need to feel informed, connected, or significant?

In a church context especially, information shared under the guise of prayer or pastoral concern can function as gossip even when every detail is accurate. "Please pray for Brother Emeka — his marriage is in trouble" may be factually true. It may still be a violation of Emeka's privacy, destructive to his reputation, and entirely unnecessary for the spiritual welfare of the person being told.

The test is not "Is this true?" It is "Is this mine to share? Does this person need to know? Will sharing this build up or tear down?"

A pastor's wife who handles information with consistent discretion — who is known to be someone things do not travel through — builds a trust that makes her far more useful to people in genuine need. People bring their real problems to those they know will not circulate them. That trust, once built, is one of the most valuable things you have.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — Overcoming Evil with Good

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el16

Romans 12 ends with one of the most demanding practical instructions in Paul's letters — a section about how believers are to respond to those who have wronged them. It immediately follows the section on mourning with those who mourn and loving without hypocrisy. Paul moves without pause from the warmth of community care to the harder territory of response to enemies. He seems to regard them as part of the same subject.

His instruction is not passive resignation. It is an active, strategic, morally demanding choice — one that requires more strength than retaliation, not less.

**TEXT:** Part 4 — Family, In-Laws, and the Pressures That Come from Outside

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el23

Not all the conflict a pastor's wife navigates comes from within the congregation. Some of the most persistent and painful pressure comes from outside — from extended family, from in-laws, from the cultural expectations of her own community of origin. These pressures are often unspoken, rarely directly addressed, and yet consistently felt: in decisions about children, about money, about where to live, about what kind of ministry wife she should be.

Joseph's story in Genesis does not speak directly to in-laws. But it is the most sustained account in the Old Testament of a person who was consistently wronged — by family, by those he served, by circumstances that seemed to contradict every promise God had made — and who came through it not bitter but enlarged. His response to his brothers at the end of the story is one of the most searching passages in the whole Bible on the subject of forgiveness and the long view.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el27
**EYEBROW:** Family Pressure
**TERM:** Extended family, in-laws, and the ministry household
**QUESTION:** How do you navigate the specific pressures that come from in-laws and extended family in a traditional Nigerian context — expectations about children, money, time, and what kind of wife and minister's wife you should be?
**ANSWER:** This is one of the areas where many pastors' wives carry weight that is rarely acknowledged in church settings — because the pressure comes from outside the church, and yet it lands directly inside the marriage and the ministry.

In many Nigerian communities, a daughter-in-law is understood to belong, in a meaningful sense, to her husband's family — not only to her husband. Her choices, her appearance, her fertility, her relationship to her own family of origin, and her involvement in ministry may all be subject to comment, expectation, and sometimes direct interference from in-laws who consider these things their legitimate concern.

When those expectations are wise and loving, they are genuinely a gift — the extended family as a resource of support, wisdom, and community. But when they conflict with a couple's sense of calling, with the wife's sense of identity, or with the basic functioning of the household, they require careful navigation.

A few principles that may help:

**The primary household is the couple's.** Genesis 2:24 — "a man shall leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife" — establishes a primary loyalty. Extended family matters enormously, but it is secondary to the marriage. A husband who allows his family of origin to consistently override his wife's voice is not honouring the marriage covenant.

**Name the pressure, don't absorb it silently.** Unspoken resentment toward in-laws tends to leak — into the marriage, into parenting, into the ministry. The couple who can talk honestly about external pressures — without one defending the family and the other attacking them — is in a much stronger position than the couple who pretend the pressure is not there.

**Choose your battles with wisdom.** Not every expectation needs to be resisted. Some things — attendance at family events, certain expressions of respect — cost little and preserve relationship. Others — decisions about where to live, how many children to have, whether the wife works outside the home — are genuinely the couple's to make. Knowing the difference matters.

**Bring it to God together.** Extended family pressure is a spiritual issue as well as a relational one. The couple that prays together about it — naming the specific pressures, asking for wisdom, seeking God's direction — is less likely to be divided by it.

This is hard territory. You are not the first pastor's wife to navigate it, and you will not be the last. But you do not have to navigate it alone — and you do not have to resolve it all at once.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch10-el34

Conflict in ministry is not a sign that something has gone wrong with your faith or your church. It is a sign that you are in close relationship with imperfect people — which is the only kind of people there are, including you.

The Bible does not promise conflict-free ministry. It promises a God who is present in the conflict, who sees the wrong done to you, who will settle accounts in his own time, and who is — even now, even in the middle of the slow fire — doing something in you that he could not do any other way.

Taiwo's three days of slow fire were not wasted. They produced a question she had not thought to ask. The question produced honesty. The honesty produced a kind of freedom she had not expected.

That is usually how it works. The conflict that feels most like an obstacle is often the thing God is most at work in. Not around it. Not despite it. In it.

Stay in the question. Let him work.
