[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el01

Hauwa had lived next door to the widow for two years before she actually saw her.

She had noticed her, of course — a small woman in her sixties, always in the same faded wrapper, collecting water from the compound tap early in the morning before anyone else was up. She knew her name was Mama Idris. She knew her husband had died three years ago and that her children were somewhere in Lagos. She had greeted her at the gate.

But one Thursday morning, returning from the market, Hauwa found Mama Idris sitting on the step outside her door, not moving. She stopped. She sat down beside her on the step. She asked nothing — just sat.

After a long time, Mama Idris said: "Today is the anniversary."

They sat together for another hour. Hauwa missed the women's prayer meeting she had been hurrying back for. She did not feel guilty about it.

That evening, thinking about it, she realised something had shifted. She had been doing a great deal of ministry — attending, organising, supporting, being present at the right events. But she had been doing most of it at a slight distance from actual people. The hour on the step with Mama Idris had felt different. Less managed. More real.

She had not known what to say. She had not needed to. She had simply been there — and that, it turned out, was the thing most needed.

This session is about pastoral care: not as a professional skill, not as a ministry programme, but as a posture of the heart. It is the capacity to see the people others overlook, to feel what they feel, to be present without agenda, and to act — concretely, consistently — on behalf of those who cannot act for themselves.

It is, the Bible suggests, close to the heart of God.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — Who Is My Neighbour?

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el03

The parable of the Good Samaritan is one of the most familiar stories in the Gospels — which makes it one of the most difficult to hear freshly. We know the outcome before we begin. The Samaritan helps. The priest and the Levite do not.

What is easily missed is the social structure of the story. The priest and the Levite are not villains. They are religious professionals with responsibilities, schedules, and legitimate reasons to be careful about ritual contamination. The Samaritan is a social outsider — ethnically and religiously suspect, someone a Jewish audience would expect to be the failure of the story, not its hero.

Jesus chooses him deliberately. And the question he ends with is not "What should you think about your neighbour?" It is: "Which one of these three acted like a neighbour?"

Neighbour, in Jesus' vocabulary, is not a category you belong to. It is something you do.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — Mourning With Those Who Mourn

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el09

Romans 12 contains one of the most concentrated passages of practical instruction in Paul's letters — a rapid sequence of short commands about how the community of God's people is to treat one another and the wider world. At its centre is a pair of phrases that together describe the most fundamental posture of genuine pastoral care.

It is not advice. It is not problem-solving. It is presence: *rejoice with those who rejoice, mourn with those who mourn.*

The mourning side of that pair is the harder one. Rejoicing with those who rejoice asks only for generosity and the absence of envy. Mourning with those who mourn asks for something more costly: the willingness to enter into someone else's grief, to sit in it with them, to resist the urge to explain it or fix it or move them through it more quickly than they are ready to go.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el14
**EYEBROW:** Worth Examining
**TERM:** "Pastoral care is the pastor's job, not mine."
**QUESTION:** Is it appropriate for a pastor's wife to be involved in pastoral care — or is that overstepping?
**ANSWER:** Pastoral care — in the broad sense of caring for the souls and bodies of people in need — is not the exclusive property of the ordained minister. It is the responsibility of the whole body of Christ.

The New Testament uses the language of "one another" more than fifty times to describe how believers are to treat each other: love one another, bear one another's burdens, encourage one another, confess to one another, pray for one another. None of these instructions is restricted to those with formal ministry roles.

A pastor's wife is, by the nature of her position, often more accessible to certain people than her husband is — particularly to women who would find it difficult to share certain things with a male pastor. The woman who has just miscarried. The wife whose marriage is struggling. The young woman who has been abused. The older woman who is dying and afraid. These women need someone — and a pastor's wife who is present, trustworthy, and genuinely caring may be the most important person in the church for them.

This does not mean taking on more than you can carry, or operating without boundaries, or substituting yourself for professional help when it is needed. Wisdom about limits is part of good pastoral care. But the instinct to withdraw from care because "that's the pastor's job" can leave some of the most vulnerable people in a congregation without anyone.

You have permission — and more than permission — to care.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — Pure Religion

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el16

James is the most practically minded writer in the New Testament. He is suspicious of faith that produces no visible fruit, of religion that fills the mouth but empties the hands. His definition of genuine religion — of what God actually considers pure and undefiled — is startling in its simplicity and its specificity. It has nothing to do with correct doctrine or impressive worship. It has to do with two categories of people: orphans and widows.

In the ancient world, and in many parts of Nigeria today, orphans and widows are among the most economically and socially vulnerable members of a community. They are people without the natural protection of a husband and father — people who depend on the generosity and justice of others for their basic survival. The prophets of Israel returned to them again and again as the test of a community's faithfulness. So does James.

**TEXT:** Part 4 — Jesus and the Widow of Nain

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el23

Luke 7 contains a brief, luminous account that is easy to pass over — a healing miracle, but unlike most of the others. The woman at the centre of it does not ask for anything. She does not reach out to touch Jesus' garment or send a message through friends or cry out from the roadside. She is simply walking — carrying her grief, heading toward a burial, not looking for anything because she has learned, perhaps, that there is nothing left to look for.

Jesus sees her. He is moved. He acts.

This encounter is a picture of what a pastoral heart looks like at its most instinctive — not responding to a request, not meeting an articulated need, but seeing someone who has not asked, and moving toward them anyway.

[type: callout; subtype: general]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el28
**EYEBROW:** A Honest Note
**TERM:** Caring without being consumed
**QUESTION:** Pastoral care involves absorbing other people's pain. How do you do that without being destroyed by it?
**ANSWER:** This is one of the most important practical questions in ministry life — and it rarely gets an honest answer.

The truth is that sustained pastoral care does cost something. You cannot sit with grief, hold trauma, witness suffering, and carry confidences without being affected. Pretending otherwise is not faith — it is denial, and it tends to produce the burnout and emotional numbness that make genuine care impossible over time.

Some things that help:

**Limit what you carry alone.** Confidentiality is important, but it does not mean carrying everything by yourself. A trusted person — a supervisor, a spiritual director, a wise friend outside the congregation — with whom you can process what you are holding without breaking confidence is not a luxury. It is essential.

**Bring it to God specifically.** Not general prayer, but bringing the specific weight of what you are carrying — the face, the situation, the grief — and leaving it with him. "I carried this today. I give it back to you now." That practice, done consistently, makes a real difference.

**Know your limits.** There are situations — severe mental illness, domestic violence, addiction, trauma — that require professional help. Knowing when to refer is not a failure of pastoral care. It is part of it. Trying to carry what you are not equipped to carry does not help the person — it puts them in the care of someone who is out of their depth, and it damages you.

**Rest between.** As Session 6 of Volume 1 established — rest is not optional. The capacity to be genuinely present to people in pain depends on having space to recover between those encounters.

You cannot pour from an empty vessel. Caring for yourself is part of caring for others.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch09-el34

Pastoral care is not a programme. It is a posture — a way of moving through the world with your eyes open and your heart available, alert to the person on the road, attentive to the woman on the step, willing to stop for the funeral procession no one expected you to notice.

It will cost you time. It will cost you margin. It will sometimes cost you the meeting you were hurrying back to. And it will ask of you something that no training fully prepares you for: the willingness to be present to pain without immediately trying to fix it.

But it is among the most Christlike things you will ever do. Jesus saw the widow of Nain before she saw him. He moved toward her before she moved toward him. He gave before she asked.

You are not Jesus. You cannot raise the dead. But you can stop. You can sit down. You can say: *I see you.* You can stay long enough for it to mean something.

That is where pastoral care begins. And sometimes, it is where it ends — and that is enough.

**TERM:** Before Session 4
**QUESTION:** What is your challenge for this week?
**ANSWER:** Two things this week.

First: identify one person in your congregation or community who is structurally vulnerable — a widow, an elderly person living alone, a woman whose husband has left, a family in genuine financial difficulty — and do one concrete thing for them. Not a prayer on their behalf from a distance. Something practical: a visit, a meal, a phone call that is long enough to actually listen, an envelope.

Second: the next time someone tells you about a difficulty they are facing — in person, by phone, in a message — resist the urge to give advice or find the silver lining. Simply say: *"That sounds really hard. Tell me more."* Then listen. Notice how long you can hold the space before the urge to fix or reassure becomes overwhelming — and notice what happens when you don't give in to it.

Bring one reflection from either of these things to the start of Session 4.
