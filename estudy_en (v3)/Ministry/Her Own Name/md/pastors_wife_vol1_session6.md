[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el01

Funke had not taken a full day off in four years.

She would have found the observation strange, if anyone had made it — because in her mind, she was not overworking. She was just being faithful. There was always something: a hospital visit, a woman who needed prayer, a children's programme to organise, a meal to cook for a grieving family, a sermon to type up, a meeting to attend alongside her husband. And when there was nothing scheduled, there was the low hum of availability — the sense that she should be ready, at any moment, to be useful.

She did not call it exhaustion. She called it service.

But her body was beginning to say otherwise. She had started forgetting things — small things, but more often than before. She snapped at her children over nothing. She sat in church on Sunday mornings and felt, beneath the singing, a numbness she did not know what to do with. She had begun to dread the week ahead on Sunday evening rather than greeting it with anything like eagerness.

If someone had suggested she needed rest — real rest, not just an earlier bedtime — she would have said: *But the work is not done. It is never done.*

And that, it turns out, is precisely the point.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — God Rested

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el03

The concept of sabbath — of a rhythm of work and rest built into the fabric of existence — does not begin with the Ten Commandments. It begins at the very beginning, in the first two chapters of the Bible, before the Fall, before sin, before human failure of any kind. God rested on the seventh day and called it holy. That pattern was written into creation before anything went wrong.

This means that rest is not a concession to human weakness. It is not a reluctant accommodation to our limitations. It is part of the original design — woven into the structure of time itself by God, who could have kept working and did not.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el07
**EYEBROW:** Worth Examining
**TERM:** "Rest is for people who are not serious about the work."
**QUESTION:** Is taking regular rest a sign of insufficient commitment to ministry?
**ANSWER:** This idea is common in high-commitment ministry cultures — and it is not from the Bible.

Jesus, in the middle of active and demanding ministry, regularly withdrew to lonely places to pray (Luke 5:16). He slept in a boat during a storm (Mark 4:38). He told his disciples, after an intense period of ministry activity, to "come away by yourselves to a desolate place and rest a while" (Mark 6:31) — and this instruction came *because* so many people were coming and going that they did not even have time to eat. Busyness was not Jesus' reason to press harder. It was his reason to withdraw.

Elijah needed sleep and food before he could hear from God (1 Kings 19). The psalmist described being led "beside still waters" and having his soul restored (Psalm 23:2–3). These are not pictures of laziness. They are pictures of a God who builds restoration into the rhythm of a faithful life.

The person who never rests is not more committed than the person who does. They are less sustainable — and over time, less useful. The work of ministry is a long-distance endeavour. It is not run at sprint pace. Those who attempt to sprint it tend not to finish well.

Rest is not the enemy of faithfulness. It is the condition of it.

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el09

Psalm 23 is perhaps the most familiar psalm in the entire Psalter — so familiar that it can be difficult to hear it freshly. But it contains one of the most striking images of God's care in all of Scripture: a shepherd who leads his sheep to still water and green pasture, not because the sheep have earned it, but because that is what a good shepherd does.

The word translated "restores" in verse 3 — *yeshobeb* in Hebrew — means to bring back, to cause to return, to turn around. It is the word used of someone who has wandered and is brought home. God does not restore us because we maintained the pace. He restores us because we are his, and restoration is what he does.

In Matthew 11, Jesus has just been speaking about the rejection of John the Baptist and the resistance of the cities that have seen his miracles. It is a chapter of disappointment and hard-heartedness. And in the middle of it, he turns and offers one of the most direct and personal invitations in the Gospels — addressed not to a crowd, not to the Pharisees, but to anyone who is weary and carrying a heavy load.

The invitation is not to try harder. It is to come.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el20
**EYEBROW:** Worth Thinking About
**TERM:** "I can rest when the children are grown / the church is bigger / things are more settled."
**QUESTION:** Is it wise to defer rest until circumstances improve?
**ANSWER:** The circumstances of ministry rarely settle in the way we imagine they will. The season of young children passes, and is replaced by the season of adolescent children. The small church plant grows, and with it the demands multiply rather than diminish. The hoped-for stability turns out to be another season of growth or difficulty with different contours.

There is no future version of your life in which rest will be easier to justify than it is now. The person who does not rest now will not suddenly find it natural to rest then. Patterns of overwork, like patterns of any kind, tend to deepen with time rather than resolve themselves.

The call to rest is not a call to wait for the right season. It is a call to build the rhythm now — imperfectly, with whatever margin you can find, in whatever form is available to you. A full sabbath day may not be possible every week in every season. But something — an hour, a morning, a practice of stopping — is almost always possible, if we are willing to treat it as the priority it actually is.

Start small. Start now. The pattern, once begun, becomes easier to protect.

**TEXT:** Part 4 — Abiding, Not Striving

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el22

John 15 contains Jesus' final extended teaching to his disciples before his arrest. He knows what is coming. And what he chooses to teach in this moment — his last opportunity for sustained instruction — is not a strategy for mission, not a plan for the church, not a list of responsibilities. It is a picture of a relationship.

He is the vine. They are the branches. The branch does not produce fruit by striving harder. It produces fruit by remaining — by staying connected to the source. The Greek word *meno*, translated "remain" or "abide," appears ten times in the first ten verses of this passage. It is the keynote of Jesus' final teaching.

Stay. Abide. Remain. Everything else follows from that.

[type: callout; subtype: general]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el27
**EYEBROW:** A Practical Note
**TERM:** What does rest actually look like?
**QUESTION:** Rest sounds simple — but for many women in ministry, the concept is so unfamiliar that they genuinely do not know what it would mean for them. What are some practical starting points?
**ANSWER:** Rest is not the same thing for everyone, and it is not only about sleep — though sleep matters enormously and is often the first thing sacrificed in ministry life.

Rest, in the fullest sense, is whatever genuinely restores you — whatever allows you to return to people and to God with more rather than less. For some women that is solitude and silence. For others it is time with close friends, or physical activity, or creative work, or time in nature. For many it is a combination.

Some starting points worth considering:

**Sabbath** — a regular, weekly rhythm of stopping. Not catching up on other tasks, not being available, not doing ministry work. Simply stopping. This looks different in different households, but the principle is the same: one day in seven belongs to rest, to God, and to renewal.

**Sleep** — most adults need seven to nine hours. Chronically short sleep impairs judgment, emotional regulation, and creativity in ways that directly affect both ministry and marriage. Protecting sleep is not indulgence — it is stewardship.

**Margin** — intentional empty space in the diary. Not every hour needs to be filled. Not every request needs a yes. The ability to say no to good things in order to protect the capacity for the most important things is a discipline, not a failure.

**Delight** — doing things simply because they are good and beautiful and enjoyable, with no ministry application. Reading for pleasure. Cooking a meal you love. Sitting outside in the evening. God made a world full of good things to be received with gratitude (1 Timothy 4:4–5). Receiving them is not a distraction from worship. It is a form of it.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch06-el33

We began Volume 1 with a question: *Who are you when you are not being useful?*

Six sessions later, here is what the Bible has said in answer.

You are a daughter of God, chosen before the world was made, known completely and loved without condition. You are more than a role — your identity is older and deeper than your husband's calling, and it cannot be earned or lost by what you do or fail to do. You are a partner in marriage, not an assistant — an *ezer*, a woman of strength. You are a jar of clay carrying extraordinary treasure, and your fragility does not disqualify you; it is part of how the light gets out. You are permitted to lament, to struggle, to say *it is enough* — and to be met there by a God who feeds before he sends. And you are a branch, not a trunk: your fruitfulness comes from remaining connected to the vine, not from working harder and longer than is humanly possible.

None of this makes the work easier. The ministry is real, the demands are real, and the people who need you are real. But the one who called you is also real — and he is not asking you to be superhuman. He is asking you to be his.

You are his. Rest in that. And come back for Volume 2.
