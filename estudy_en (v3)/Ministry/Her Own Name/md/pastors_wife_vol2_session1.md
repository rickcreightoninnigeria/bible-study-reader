[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch07-el01

They had been at the same denominational women's conference, sitting at the same table, and within twenty minutes Yetunde knew they had almost nothing in common — except the role.

Across from her was Sister Grace: dignified, softly spoken, impeccably dressed, present at every church function and never the centre of it. She taught the women's Sunday school class, visited the sick on Wednesday afternoons, and was widely regarded as the ideal pastor's wife. When asked about her gifts, she said simply: "I support my husband." She said it without resentment or embarrassment. It was the truest thing she knew about herself.

Beside Yetunde was Pastor Ngozi's wife — Chiamaka — who ran a legal aid clinic for women out of the church compound three days a week, preached in the women's service twice a month, led a Bible study for market women on Friday mornings, and had, the previous year, started a network connecting church planters' wives across three states. She was expansive, opinionated, and clearly uncomfortable sitting still for very long.

Both women loved Jesus. Both had faithful marriages. Both were serving their churches and their communities. And yet they could not have been more different.

Who was right? Who was the real pastor's wife?

The answer the Bible gives is: both of them. And neither of them has the right to insist the other one should look more like herself.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — Many Gifts, One Spirit

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch07-el03

The New Testament's teaching on spiritual gifts is one of the most liberating — and most neglected — resources for thinking about the role of a pastor's wife. It insists, repeatedly and with considerable emphasis, that God distributes different gifts to different people according to his own purposes — not according to their gender, their husband's title, or the expectations of their church community.

Paul makes this case most clearly in two letters: Romans 12 and 1 Corinthians 12. Both passages begin by insisting on diversity as part of God's design — not a problem to be managed, but a feature to be celebrated.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch07-el09
**EYEBROW:** Worth Examining
**TERM:** "All pastors' wives should lead the women's ministry."
**QUESTION:** Is there a standard set of roles that every pastor's wife should fulfil?
**ANSWER:** No — and the pressure to conform to a single model of the pastor's wife is one of the most widespread and least examined sources of unhappiness in ministry marriages.

The model varies from denomination to denomination, from culture to culture, and even from congregation to congregation — which is itself a clue that it is culturally constructed rather than biblically mandated. In some traditions, the pastor's wife is expected to lead worship. In others, she must never be seen on the platform. In some churches she runs the women's ministry. In others she is expected to stay invisible and domestic. The expectations are often mutually contradictory — which means they cannot all be right.

What the Bible does require of a pastor's wife is what it requires of every believer: love God, love others, use the gifts you have been given, grow in character, and serve faithfully in the ways that are available to you. Beyond that, the shape of her ministry is between her, her husband, and God — and the congregation's preferences, however sincerely held, do not override that.

This does not mean ignoring the community or being needlessly provocative. Wisdom about timing and context always matters. But the starting question is not "What does this congregation expect?" It is "What has God given me, and where is he calling me to use it?"

**TEXT:** Part 2 — Women Who Led

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch07-el11

The Old and New Testaments together give us a wide range of women in significant roles — not as exceptions or anomalies, but as part of the normal landscape of God's people. Their stories resist the flattening of women's ministry into a single permitted shape.

Two figures in particular are worth pausing over: Deborah, a judge and military leader in Israel's most difficult period; and Priscilla, a teacher and church planter in the early church. Neither of them fits neatly into the category of "supporter." Both are fully themselves, fully gifted, and fully used by God.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — The Quieter Calling

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch07-el18

Everything said so far about women's gifts and leadership could leave a different woman feeling unseen — the woman whose gifts are quieter, whose ministry is less visible, whose calling is genuinely expressed through faithfulness in the background rather than prominence in the foreground.

She needs to hear something clearly: the Bible does not rank gifts by visibility. The body needs every part. And the consistent testimony of the New Testament is that the most quietly exercised gifts — mercy, service, hospitality, encouragement — are not lesser. They are the ones without which the body cannot function at all.

Sister Grace, from our opening story, was not failing to fulfil her potential. She was fulfilling it — in her own shape, according to her own gifts, at the level of depth and consistency that made her genuinely irreplaceable to the people around her. The problem would only arise if she used her own calling as a template to judge Chiamaka — or if Chiamaka used hers to look down on Grace.

Both errors are alive and well in the church. Both are worth resisting.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch07-el21
**EYEBROW:** Family Pressure
**TERM:** "In our family, women don't take leadership positions."
**QUESTION:** What do you do when your family background or ethnic tradition has strong expectations about what women should and should not do — and those expectations conflict with the gifts and calling you sense from God?
**ANSWER:** This is one of the most genuinely difficult tensions in the lives of many Nigerian Christian women — and it deserves a careful answer rather than a quick one.

Family traditions about women's roles are not always wrong. Many carry real wisdom about honour, community, and relationship. The instinct to respect elders, to maintain family harmony, and to avoid causing unnecessary offence is often a good instinct, and the Bible affirms it in many places.

But family tradition is not Scripture. And when the two conflict — when a family's cultural expectations would require a woman to suppress or deny gifts that God has clearly given her — the conflict needs to be named honestly, not dissolved by simply giving way.

This does not mean acting provocatively or without wisdom. It means that a woman's first accountability is to God, who gave her her gifts and her calling. Family expectations — even very strong ones, even from people she loves and respects — are secondary to that. Her husband is her primary human partner in discerning how to navigate the tension. Extended family, however influential, do not have the authority to define her calling.

These conversations are rarely easy. They may need to happen slowly, over time, with patience and love on all sides. But they need to happen — because gifts suppressed for long enough do not stay quietly dormant. They tend to surface in other ways, and not always constructively.

The practical question this session raises is not only theological. It is personal: *What are your gifts? What is your calling? What is the shape of the ministry God has given specifically to you — not to the idealised pastor's wife, not to the woman at the next table at the conference, but to you?*

These questions deserve more than a moment's thought. They deserve the kind of sustained, honest reflection that most women in ministry rarely give themselves permission to undertake. The following questions are designed to help you begin that reflection — not to produce a final answer, but to start a conversation with God and perhaps with one or two trusted others.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch07-el32

There is no single template for a pastor's wife in the pages of the New Testament. There is no job description, no uniform, no required gift set. There are women who prophesy and women who serve. Women who teach men and women who teach children. Women who travel and women who stay. Women whose names are listed first and women whose names are not recorded at all.

What they share is not a role. It is a Person — Jesus, in whom they have found their identity, their worth, and their calling. And from that shared foundation, they go in many different directions, with many different gifts, into many different corners of the work.

You are one of those women. God placed specific gifts in you, in your particular combination, for his particular purposes — not the purposes of the denomination, not the expectations of the congregation, not the traditions of your family of origin. His purposes. Yours.

The question is not whether you fit the template. There is no template. The question is whether you are becoming, faithfully and specifically, the woman he made you to be.

**EYEBROW:** Application Challenge
**TERM:** Before Session 2
**QUESTION:** What is your challenge for this week?
**ANSWER:** Two things.

First: take the gifts question seriously this week. Set aside an hour — with a notebook, or in prayer, or both — and work through these three questions as honestly as you can:
- What do I do that seems to come naturally to me, but that others find genuinely difficult?
- What activities leave me energised rather than depleted, even when they are demanding?
- What do people consistently come to me for — not because I am the pastor's wife, but because of who I specifically am?

If you are not sure of your answers, ask someone who knows you well and will tell you the truth.

Second: if you are in a group, come prepared to share one gift you believe God has given you — not a role you have been assigned, but a genuine gift. Say it plainly, without apology. Practise receiving it when others affirm it.

Bring both to the start of Session 2.
