[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch03-el01

Blessing had been married to Pastor Emeka for eight years. She loved him. She respected his calling. She prayed for him daily, covered for him when he was exhausted, and had learned to read the signs that told her when he needed space and when he needed someone to talk to.

But there were things she never said. Not because he had forbidden it — he had not — but because she had learned, gradually and without quite noticing, to make herself smaller. If she had an opinion about the direction of the church, she kept it to herself. If she was struggling, she waited until he had time — which was rarely. If she disagreed with a decision he had made, she would mention it once, gently, and then let it go.

She told herself this was submission. She told herself a good pastor's wife makes her husband's ministry easier, not harder.

What she had not yet asked was whether this was what God actually intended — or whether she and Emeka had both quietly settled for something less than a marriage.

This session is about marriage. Not marriage as a role to perform, but marriage as a relationship God designed — with a specific shape, a specific purpose, and a depth that ministry can either deepen or quietly hollow out, depending on how you tend it.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — Submission, Love, and What Both Actually Mean

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch03-el09

Ephesians 5 is perhaps the most discussed — and most misused — passage about marriage in the New Testament. It contains the word *submission*, which in some contexts has been used to justify silence, passivity, and even the endurance of harm. It deserves careful reading.

What is often missed is that Paul's instruction to wives comes in the middle of a much larger instruction to *all* believers: "Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ" (v21). Mutual submission is the foundation. What follows is not a hierarchy of power but a description of how love expresses itself differently for a husband and a wife — and the standard Paul sets for husbands is extraordinarily demanding.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch03-el14
**EYEBROW:** Cultural Pressure
**TERM:** "A pastor's wife must always agree with her husband in public."
**QUESTION:** Is it disloyal — or even unspiritual — for a pastor's wife to disagree with her husband?
**ANSWER:** Loyalty and honesty are not the same thing — and a good marriage requires both.

A wife who agrees with everything her husband says and never raises a concern is not being a faithful partner. She is being a mirror. And a mirror cannot help a man see his blind spots, course-correct before a mistake becomes a crisis, or grow into a wiser leader.

Proverbs 27:17 says: "Iron sharpens iron, and one person sharpens another." That picture assumes friction. Sharpening does not happen when two soft surfaces touch.

In many traditional Nigerian contexts, there is a strong expectation that a pastor's wife will be visibly supportive and publicly agreeable — that any sign of disagreement, even in private, would dishonour her husband or undermine his authority. This expectation is worth examining carefully.

The biblical picture of a good wife — including in Proverbs 31 — is not one of silent compliance. It is one of active wisdom, capable judgment, and shared responsibility. Her husband trusts her *precisely because* she brings something real to the partnership.

Disagreement, raised with respect and love, in the right time and place, is an act of faithfulness — to your husband, and to God.

1 Peter 3 addresses wives in a specific and difficult situation — some of the women Peter was writing to were married to unbelieving husbands, men who were not following Christ. ---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch03-el21

The final passage for this session has been used in many ways — sometimes to celebrate women, sometimes to burden them, sometimes both at once. Proverbs 31 describes a woman of strong character, wide capability, and genuine influence. She is active in commerce, generous to the poor, wise in counsel, and trusted completely by her husband.

She is not his assistant. She is his partner — with a sphere of her own, strengths of her own, and a reputation that stands independently alongside his.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch03-el26
**EYEBROW:** Worth Thinking About
**TERM:** "My husband's calling is more important than mine."
**QUESTION:** Is it true that a pastor's calling takes priority over his wife's gifts and sense of calling?
**ANSWER:** God calls individuals — not titles, and not couples as a single unit with one calling between them. When a man is called to pastoral ministry, that call is on him. It does not automatically define what his wife is called to do, what gifts she should exercise, or how she should spend her time and energy.

In practice, a couple must make real decisions together about how to steward two lives, two sets of gifts, and one household. Those decisions involve genuine sacrifice and negotiation on both sides. But sacrifice chosen freely, in love, is very different from sacrifice assumed automatically — where the wife's gifts, ambitions, and sense of calling are treated as background noise to the main event of her husband's ministry.

God placed gifts in you before your husband was called to ministry. Those gifts did not become less significant when you married. They are still his gift to you and, through you, to others.

This does not mean a pastor's wife should not support her husband's ministry — of course she should, as he should support hers. But support flows in both directions in a healthy marriage. A husband who is secure in his calling will not feel threatened by a wife who has a calling of her own.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch03-el27
**EYEBROW:** Family Pressure
**TERM:** "In our culture, a wife does not challenge her husband."
**QUESTION:** What do you do when cultural expectations about a wife's role conflict with what the Bible actually teaches about marriage?
**ANSWER:** In many Nigerian communities — across ethnic groups and religious traditions — a wife is expected to defer to her husband in all things, to speak only when invited to, and to express disagreement only indirectly, if at all. These expectations are often deeply felt and socially enforced. They may come from family, from community elders, from the wider church culture, or from patterns modelled across generations.

It is important to treat these expectations with respect — they are not always wrong, and they often carry genuine wisdom about the value of honour, loyalty, and restraint. But cultural tradition is not the same as biblical teaching, and the two do not always agree.

The Bible's vision of marriage is one of mutual love, mutual service, and genuine partnership between two people who are equally heirs of God's grace (1 Peter 3:7). That vision may be in tension with certain cultural patterns — and when it is, the Bible's vision takes priority.

This does not mean acting in ways that are unnecessarily provocative or that bring genuine harm to a family relationship. Wisdom matters. Timing matters. But it does mean that a pastor's wife has both the right and the responsibility — before God — to bring her whole self to her marriage: her voice, her wisdom, her concerns, and her gifts.

She is not a lesser partner. She is a fellow heir.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch03-el33

Marriage in ministry is both a gift and a pressure. It can be the place where you are most known, most supported, and most yourself — or it can become one more performance, one more role to maintain, the closest relationship slowly hollowed out by busyness and unspoken needs.

God did not design it that way. He designed it for *one flesh* — for the kind of union where two people become genuinely more together than they were apart. Where a man's calling is sharpened by a woman's wisdom. Where a woman's gifts are honoured and given room to grow. Where both partners are known, and neither is alone.

That marriage is worth fighting for — not against each other, but together, against everything that tries to pull you apart.

You are not your husband's assistant. You are his partner, his equal before God, and — in the best and fullest sense — his *ezer*: a helper of strength, not of silence.

**QUESTION:** What is your challenge for this week?
**ANSWER:** Choose one thing this week that is just for your marriage — not for the ministry, not for the congregation, not for the children. Something that is only for you and your husband.

It does not need to be elaborate. A walk. A meal without phones. A conversation about something other than church business. An honest question: *"How are you — really?"* And the same question turned back towards you, with enough time to answer it properly.

If that kind of conversation feels awkward or unfamiliar, that itself is worth noticing. What has crowded it out? And what would you need to do differently to protect it?
