[type: text; subtype: intro]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el01

Amina had been in ministry long enough to know that no one talked about this part.

The sermons preached in their church were full of faith and victory. The testimony meetings rang with stories of breakthrough and blessing. The women in the fellowship smiled and praised God and spoke of his goodness — and it was all true, as far as it went.

But Amina was in her third year of what she could only describe as a dry season. Not a crisis — nothing dramatic had happened. Her husband's church plant was growing slowly, much more slowly than they had hoped. Two families they had poured themselves into had left without explanation. A woman she had discipled for eighteen months had turned against her after a misunderstanding that still made no sense. She had been sleeping badly for months. She had stopped enjoying prayer. She felt, most days, like she was going through the motions — and she felt guilty for feeling that way, because she knew she was supposed to feel grateful.

She had not told anyone. Pastors' wives don't say these things. Or so she had been taught, by everything she had observed around her.

This session says otherwise. Difficulty, dryness, loneliness, and grief are not signs of weak faith. They are part of the landscape of a life faithfully lived — and the Bible is far more honest about them than most churches are.

**TEXT:** Part 1 — The Psalms Give Us Permission

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el03

The book of Psalms is the prayer book of the Bible — used by Israel in worship for centuries, quoted by Jesus on the cross, sung in the early church. It is also, startlingly, full of complaint, lament, confusion, and raw grief. Roughly a third of the psalms are laments — prayers that begin not with praise, but with a question or a cry.

The fact that these prayers are in the Bible is itself a statement: God is not offended by honest distress. He is not embarrassed by our confusion. He does not require us to perform contentment we do not feel. He invites the real thing.

Psalm 13 is one of the shortest laments in the Psalter. It is also one of the most complete, moving in just six verses from desolation to trust — not because the circumstances have changed, but because the psalmist has brought them honestly before God.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el08
**EYEBROW:** Worth Examining
**TERM:** "If I were stronger in faith, I wouldn't feel this way."
**QUESTION:** Is depression, exhaustion, or spiritual dryness a sign of weak faith?
**ANSWER:** This is one of the most damaging things believed in many Nigerian church contexts — and it is not supported by the Bible.

Elijah, one of the greatest prophets in Israel's history, sat under a tree after his greatest ministry victory and asked God to take his life (1 Kings 19:4). He was exhausted, afraid, and done. God's response was not rebuke. It was food, water, sleep, and the gentle question: *"What are you doing here, Elijah?"* — asked not in judgment, but in care.

David, described as "a man after God's own heart," wrote Psalms of anguish, confusion, and despair. Jeremiah is sometimes called the weeping prophet. Paul described being "under great pressure, far beyond our ability to endure, so that we despaired of life itself" (2 Corinthians 1:8). These are not people of weak faith. They are among the most faithful people in the entire Bible.

Mental and emotional exhaustion are not spiritual failures. They are human realities — realities that God meets with compassion, not condemnation. If you are struggling with persistent low mood, exhaustion that sleep does not fix, loss of pleasure in things that once brought joy, or thoughts that frighten you, please speak to someone — a trusted friend, a pastor, or a medical professional. These things deserve care, not concealment.

**TEXT:** Part 2 — Jars of Clay

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el10

Paul writes 2 Corinthians from a position of considerable suffering. He has been beaten, imprisoned, shipwrecked, and rejected. He is writing to a church that has questioned his authority and doubted his apostleship — partly, it seems, because he does not look impressive enough. He is not a polished orator. He carries the marks of a hard life. He does not project strength.

His response is one of the most remarkable passages in the New Testament — not a defence of his credentials, but a reframing of what ministry actually is. Weakness, he argues, is not an embarrassment to the gospel. It is the condition under which the gospel is most clearly displayed.

**TEXT:** Part 3 — Elijah Under the Tree

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el16

The story of Elijah's collapse in 1 Kings 19 is one of the most psychologically honest passages in the Old Testament. It follows immediately after one of the greatest acts of prophetic ministry in Israel's history — the confrontation with the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel (1 Kings 18). Elijah has just experienced extraordinary power and extraordinary vindication.

And then Jezebel sends a single threatening message, and he falls apart.

This sequence matters. Burnout, collapse, and despair do not always follow failure. They often follow great effort — sometimes the greatest effort. The crash after the mountain is a real pattern, and God's response to it in this passage is one of the most tender things in all of Scripture.

[type: callout; subtype: misunderstanding]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el21
**EYEBROW:** Cultural Pressure
**TERM:** "Pastors' wives don't talk about these things."
**QUESTION:** What happens when there is no safe space for a pastor's wife to be honest about her struggles?
**ANSWER:** In many church contexts — particularly in traditional or hierarchical settings — there is an unwritten rule that those in leadership do not show weakness. A pastor's wife who admits to loneliness, depression, marital strain, or spiritual dryness risks being seen as a liability to her husband's ministry, a disappointment to the congregation, or simply someone who "doesn't have enough faith."

The result is a particular kind of loneliness: being surrounded by people, and profoundly alone.

This is not a small problem. Concealed struggles do not tend to stay concealed — they grow. And when they eventually surface, they often do so in ways that are far more damaging than honest disclosure would have been.

The Bible models something very different. Paul shares his anguish with the churches he serves. The psalmists publish their laments. Elijah's collapse is not hidden in a footnote — it is given an entire chapter. The message is consistent: honesty before God and before trusted others is not weakness. It is the condition of genuine health.

Every pastor's wife deserves at least one person — ideally outside her own congregation — to whom she can say the real thing and be received without judgment. If you do not have that person, finding one is not a luxury. It is a necessity.

**TEXT:** Part 4 — The God of All Comfort

---

[type: text; subtype: bridge]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el23

Paul opens 2 Corinthians — the letter in which he is most honest about his own suffering — not with complaint, but with a doxology. Before he describes what he has been through, he names who God has been to him in it. And the name he reaches for is not "God of power" or "God of victory." It is something quieter and more personal: *the God of all comfort.*

What follows is not just a personal testimony. It is a theology of suffering — an account of why difficulty, when it is met by God's comfort, does not merely end in relief, but becomes a resource for others.

[type: text; subtype: closing]
**ELEMENT ID:** ch05-el33

You are not the first pastor's wife to sit under a tree and say: *It is enough. I have had enough.*

You are not the first to feel forgotten, or to pray and hear nothing, or to give and give and find yourself running on empty. You are not the first to smile on Sunday and weep on Tuesday. You are not the first to wonder whether anyone sees what this actually costs.

Elijah was not the first either. And God did not rebuke him. He fed him, let him sleep, and asked a quiet question. Then he sent him back — not to the same thing, but to the next thing. And it turned out he was not as alone as he thought.

You are not as alone as you think. There are seven thousand you don't know about. And the God who fed Elijah under the juniper tree, who comforted Paul in his imprisonments, who received the psalmist's raw and unpolished grief — that God sees you. All of you. Even the parts you have been afraid to show.

Especially those parts.

**QUESTION:** What is your challenge for this week?
**ANSWER:** Two things this week — and you choose the order.

First: write a lament. Not a polished prayer — a real one. Take a page and write to God about whatever is actually hard right now. Use the structure of Psalm 13 if it helps: *How long...? / Look on me and answer... / But I trust...* You do not have to reach the trust part in one sitting. The point is to say the real thing.

Second: identify one person — outside your congregation if possible — to whom you could say something true about how you are actually doing. Not the full story, necessarily. Just something real. A text, a phone call, a cup of tea. The beginning of the conversation Amina had never allowed herself to have.

Bring whatever you are willing to share from these two things to the start of Session 6.
